# Prediction-Market Forecasting Edge: A Sourced Playbook for a Read-Only Forecast Lab

## TL;DR
- **Real, documented edge is narrow and category-specific, not general.** The strongest evidence-backed edges for beating a Polymarket price are: (1) *model-drivable* markets where an external quantitative model exists (weather via NWS/ECMWF, macro data via nowcasts, election structure via poll models); (2) *systematic recalibration* of the market price itself (extremizing away from 50% at long horizons, where markets are provably underconfident); and (3) *logical-consistency* corrections across linked/negRisk markets. Superforecaster-style disciplined aggregation beats markets by a modest but real margin (roughly 20–30% lower error in specific domains).
- **A large class of markets is structurally unpredictable and should be cut early:** short-horizon crypto/asset-price markets (martingale-like), single-stochastic-event novelty markets ("will X tweet Y"), and thickly-traded near-resolution favorites already at 95–99¢. Frontier LLMs alone still *trail* both superforecasters (best-LLM Brier ~0.101 vs ~0.081) and the market crowd, so an LLM-only pipeline should be treated as one noisy input, not the edge itself.
- **For the lab, prioritize by evidence × implementability × sample size:** weather and macro-data markets (highest model edge, huge monthly sample), long-horizon political/geopolitical markets (exploit documented underconfidence via extremizing), and cross-market consistency checks. Detecting a realistic 1–3 Brier-point skill edge requires **hundreds to low-thousands of resolved markets per category**, so statistical power, not cleverness, is the binding constraint.

## Key Findings

**1. The favorite-longshot bias on Polymarket is REVERSED, and it is small.** Unlike horse racing (where longshots are overbet), the largest Polymarket dataset (Qin & Yang, arXiv 2606.04217; 1.20 billion trades / 1.30 million markets / $61B volume, Nov 2022–Apr 2026) finds a "favorite-longshot reversal": low-price tokens are *overpriced* and high-price tokens *underpriced*. Mean returns (payout − price) by decile from their Table 3: the 0.00–0.10 decile returns −0.0023 (overpriced), the sign flips around 0.30–0.40 (−0.0005 → +0.0026), and the 0.80–0.90 decile returns +0.0098 (most underpriced), with the top decile at +0.0056. A separate SSRN study (Reichenbach & Walther, 5910522; 478 million trades) finds "no evidence of a general longshot bias" and that "market prices closely track realized probabilities and slightly outperform bookmaker odds." **Interpretation:** the raw magnitude (under one cent on average) is too small to trade profitably after fees/spread, but it is directionally exploitable as a recalibration prior — nudge cheap tokens down and expensive tokens up.

**2. Markets are systematically UNDERCONFIDENT far from resolution — the single most exploitable documented calibration error.** Le (arXiv 2602.19520; 292 million trades / 327,000 contracts across Kalshi + Polymarket) decomposes calibration and finds a "universal horizon effect": mean calibration slope rises from 0.99 within one hour of resolution to 1.32 beyond one month. Prices are compressed toward 50% at long horizons — a 70¢ contract one month out corresponds to a true probability closer to 75%. In **politics** the effect is strongest and persists at nearly all horizons: a 70¢ political contract one week out corresponds to ~83% true probability. This is a direct mandate for **extremizing** long-horizon favorite prices.

**3. Domain calibration differs sharply — the branch-cutting map is empirical.** From Le (2602.19520): **Politics** = chronic underconfidence (extremize favorites; slopes 0.93–1.83). **Sports** = well-calibrated at 0–48h (slopes 0.90–1.10) but underconfident beyond a month (1.74). **Weather** = *overconfident* at short horizons (slopes 0.69–0.97, prices too extreme) — meaning short-horizon weather prices overstate certainty and can be corrected with proper forecast-uncertainty modeling. **Entertainment** = overconfident (−0.09 intercept). Large political trades on Kalshi are *more* compressed (slope 1.74 vs 1.19 for single-contract trades), consistent with partisan "bilateral cancellation" pulling prices toward 50%; this large-trade effect does not replicate on Polymarket (Δ = +0.11, not significant).

**4. Superforecasters beat markets by a real but modest margin; LLMs do not yet.** The Good Judgment Project beat its control group by more than 50% (IARPA program manager Steven Rieber called it "the largest improvement in judgmental forecasting accuracy observed in the literature") and superforecasters "outperformed those of the intelligence community by about 30 percent" — i.e., analysts with classified access. Good Judgment reports superforecasters were 66% more accurate than Fed futures on FOMC inflection points historically; FT data journalist Joel Suss found they "proved 30% more accurate on average than the futures in 2024-2025," adding this "is looking less fluky as time goes on and the data piles up." On ForecastBench (Oct 2025), superforecasters lead with a difficulty-adjusted Brier of 0.081 vs the best LLM (GPT-4.5) at 0.101 — roughly a 20% edge; the Forecasting Research Institute projects LLM–superforecaster parity around late 2026–2027 (95% CI spanning Dec 2025–2028). On Metaculus's AI Benchmark, pro forecasters still beat the top-10 bot team with strong significance and the gap has not narrowed over four quarters (head-to-head ~ −20 in Q2 2025).

**5. LLMs help most as an ensembled input, not a standalone.** Halawi et al. (NeurIPS 2024, "Approaching Human-Level Forecasting with Language Models") built a retrieval-augmented system that "nears the crowd aggregate" and, when combined with the crowd (4× weight to crowd), beats either alone; it especially helps on uncertain questions (crowd Brier 0.24 in the 0.3–0.7 band). Metaculus found "models matter more than prompts" and that aggregating multiple forecasts plus iterating on scaffolding is the reliable improvement lever.

**6. Extremizing aggregated forecasts is well-established.** Baron et al. (2014, "Two Reasons to Make Aggregated Probability Forecasts More Extreme") and Satopää et al. (2014) show extremized crowd averages beat raw averages; Satopää found an optimal extremizing factor in the range 1.16–3.92 on geopolitical questions. Caveat: extremizing can backfire when forecasters share a biased prior, and the optimal factor risks overfitting if learned from limited history.

**7. Whale/manipulation episodes create measurable, but hard-to-exploit, mispricing windows.** Per Bloomberg (citing Chainalysis), the "French whale" — best known by username Fredi9999, later self-identified as "Théo" — used as many as 11 accounts, raised his stake from an initial ~$30M to ~$80M, and netted more than $85M; $3.7B was wagered on the race on Polymarket. His concentrated buying pushed Polymarket's Trump probability 10–15 percentage points above competitor platforms during October 2024 (SSRN 6670638, "The Polymarket Paradox"). Polymarket's final ~57% Trump was more accurate than polls, but the episode shows a single actor can create a persistent cross-platform gap — a *forecast signal* (fade the divergence toward the consensus of other venues) more than a clean arbitrage.

**8. Resolution-criteria and UMA dispute risk are a genuine, recurring mispricing source.** The Zelensky "suit" market drew $200M+ in volume (some report $242M), initially resolved "Yes," then flipped to "No" via UMA governance amid manipulation allegations — a market on a subjective, poorly-defined criterion. A separate $217,000 Astros–Dodgers market was mis-resolved. "The Polymarket Paradox" notes two 2025 oracle disputes totaling >$250M settled by governance votes. **Implication:** markets with subjective/ambiguous resolution criteria carry irreducible settlement risk and should be flagged or excluded.

**9. Logical-consistency violations across linked markets are documented and quantified.** Polymarket's negRisk (mutually-exclusive) events should have prices summing to 1. Saguillo, Ghafouri, Kiffer & Suárez-Tangil ("Unravelling the Probabilistic Forest," arXiv 2508.03474, Aug 2025), analyzing 86 million bets across 8,659 single-condition and 1,578 multiple-condition markets (17,218 conditions total, Apr 2024–Apr 2025), documented "Total realized arbitrage across all strategies: $39,587,585." NegRisk market rebalancing is reported to generate the bulk of profits from a small share of opportunities. For a *read-only forecast lab*, these violations are a free calibration signal: when a mutually-exclusive set sums to >1, deflate proportionally; conditional-probability violations (P(A∧B) vs P(A)·P(B|A)) flag mispriced legs.

**10. Cross-platform lead-lag is informative.** For the week ending March 1, 2026 (DeFi Rate tracker), Polymarket volume surged 31.9% to $2.40B while Kalshi posted $2.73B, and Kalshi's share of combined Kalshi+Polymarket volume fell from 58.7% to 53.2%. The two venues diverge most around economic data releases, policy-announcement windows, and crypto news. Polymarket's deeper political/crypto liquidity generally leads price discovery there, but can *lag* Kalshi during sudden moves. Divergences persist because capital cannot move frictionlessly between USD (Kalshi) and USDC-on-Polygon (Polymarket) — so the gap is a real signal, not just noise.

## Details

### Which categories are PREDICTABLE (keep) vs PURE NOISE (cut)

**KEEP — model-drivable, high edge:**
- **Weather (Kalshi/Polymarket temperature & precip):** The canonical documented edge. Per NOAA NESDIS, a seven-day forecast is accurate about 80% of the time and a five-day forecast roughly 90% (accuracy defined as high-temp within ~3°F, per the Washington Post's July 2024 analysis of first-ever released NWS data). Retail traders anchor on round numbers and misprice forecast uncertainty; Le (2602.19520) confirms weather markets are *overconfident* at short horizons. Practitioner tools blend NWS/GFS/HRRR/ECMWF ensembles into a probability distribution and act on gaps of ≥8 percentage points. Model-drivable, high-frequency, huge-sample.
- **Macro data releases (CPI, NFP, Fed, GDP):** The Atlanta Fed reports that since 2011, final GDPNow forecasts have an average absolute error of 0.77pp and RMSE of 1.17pp (2011:Q3–2025:Q2), roughly ~1.1pp MAE 90 days out narrowing to ~0.5pp just before release — matching the Blue Chip/SPF consensus and beating naive statistical benchmarks. Edge comes from ingesting model output faster and mapping it to threshold probabilities before retail catches up. Cross-platform divergence is largest here.
- **Long-horizon elections/politics:** Not because you predict better than the crowd, but because the crowd is provably *underconfident* (compressed toward 50%) far from resolution. Extremizing the favorite is the mechanical edge. Poll-based structural models (FiveThirtyEight-style) are a useful second input; historically markets and models trade blows, and combining them (plus economic fundamentals) reduces error because their errors are uncorrelated.

**CUT — structurally unpredictable:**
- **Short-horizon crypto/asset-price markets (e.g., "BTC above $X at 4pm"):** These track a martingale-like underlying. At hourly frequency, no model beats the random walk (weak-form efficiency holds); the current price already embeds all public signal. Coin-flip branches with no forecasting foothold — the highest-priority cut.
- **Single-stochastic-event novelty markets ("will X tweet/say Y," "will Jesus return in 2025"):** Resolution depends on one idiosyncratic event with no information flow and often ambiguous criteria. No reference class, high settlement risk.
- **Near-resolution favorites already at 95–99¢:** The under-convergence gap is real per Le's horizon effect, but the residual edge is sub-cent, and fees/spread consume it. Not worth lab attention.
- **Efficient short-horizon sports:** Major-league sides are weak-form efficient; one study of 155,000+ North American games over 16 seasons found no long-term profit from simple odds-based strategies. Beating the closing line requires genuine private modeling edge. A 2024 *Management Science* paper did identify *some* exploitable overreaction in MLB line movements, so niche sub-markets are not hopeless — but this is a low-priority, high-difficulty branch.

### The liquidity-efficiency relationship (counterintuitive)
Qin & Yang find that markets with *wider* spreads exhibit *lower* forecast errors — "a selection effect reflecting that high-spread niche markets attract informed specialists rather than retail noise traders." (In their microstructure results, higher Gibbs spread negatively predicts Brier scores.) This inverts the usual intuition. For the lab: thin/wide-spread markets are not automatically "dumb money" opportunities; some are dominated by specialists whose prices are sharp. Volume alone is a poor proxy for exploitability.

### Skill exists but is concentrated
Reichenbach & Walther find fewer than one-third of Polymarket traders earn positive profits, but the share of traders with positive gains-per-bet exceeds chance and profits persist over time — evidence of genuine skill, with skilled traders "exploiting the biases of less skilled participants." Their calibration inaccuracies cluster "early in a contract's lifecycle and close to resolution."

### Overreaction / underreaction to news
Prediction markets react fast but imperfectly. Case studies (e.g., LiveTradeBench on the Russia-Ukraine ceasefire market) show both overreaction and grounded reaction to the same news stream depending on framing. The documented underconfidence-at-long-horizons pattern is itself a form of chronic underreaction to the strength of available evidence.

### Statistical power — the binding constraint
Detecting a realistic skill edge of 1–3 Brier points is hard. Analogues from the validation literature: external-validation guidance requires ~100 events + 100 non-events to detect a 0.1 C-statistic difference at 80% power; AUC differences of 0.02 need ~900–3,700 per group. ForecastBench itself waits ~50 days of predictions to reliably rank a model. **Practical rule:** to detect a ~2 Brier-point edge with confidence, plan on the order of hundreds-to-low-thousands of resolved markets *per category*, use paired tests (your forecast vs market on the same market), and never pool across categories with different base rates — the Brier score is not comparable across populations, and even a perfect model yields a non-zero Brier under irreducible outcome variance.

### Known failure modes to engineer against
- **Look-ahead / news leakage:** LLM retrieval must be time-boxed to the forecast timestamp; Halawi et al. restricted test questions to after the model knowledge cutoff to avoid contamination.
- **Selection bias in which markets get forecast:** freezing forecasts only on markets you find "interesting" biases the score. Pre-register the universe.
- **Overfitting to resolved history:** extremizing factors and recalibration slopes learned on past data can overfit; use out-of-sample validation and conservative shrinkage.
- **Resolution ambiguity:** subjective-criteria markets (Zelensky suit) can flip on governance; exclude or heavily discount.

## Recommendations

**Stage 1 (build now — highest evidence × sample × implementability):**
1. **Weather markets** as the flagship pipeline: ingest NWS/ECMWF/GFS/HRRR ensembles, build a per-city temperature distribution, and compare to market-implied bucket probabilities. Log forecasts only where your model uncertainty is genuinely tighter than the market's implied spread. Huge monthly sample → fastest path to statistically detectable skill.
2. **Macro-data markets** (CPI/NFP/Fed/GDP): wire in GDPNow and equivalent nowcasts; map model output to threshold probabilities. Benchmark against both Kalshi and Polymarket.
3. **Systematic recalibration layer:** apply a horizon-dependent extremizing transform (start conservative, ~1.2–1.5, calibrated to Le's slopes) to long-horizon political favorites; deflate cheap-token prices per the reversal finding.

**Stage 2 (add once Stage 1 shows measured skill):**
4. **Cross-market consistency engine:** auto-detect negRisk sums ≠ 1 and conditional-probability violations; use them to adjust individual leg forecasts.
5. **Cross-platform divergence signal:** when Polymarket and Kalshi disagree by more than a set threshold on the same event, treat the higher-liquidity/leading venue as the prior and flag the lagging one.
6. **LLM ensemble as one input:** run a retrieval-augmented, time-boxed LLM forecaster (multiple models, trimmed-mean aggregation) and blend it with the market price and any structural model — weighting the crowd/market heavily, per Halawi.

**Stage 3 (research-grade):**
7. Long-horizon geopolitics with superforecaster-style decomposition (base rates → Fermi-ization → frequent small updates → extremized aggregation).

**Benchmarks / thresholds that change the plan:**
- If a category's paired Brier vs market is not beating the market by ≥1 point after ~300+ resolved markets, cut it.
- If LLM-standalone Brier stays >0.10 and above the market, keep it strictly as an ensemble minority input.
- Re-evaluate the extremizing factor quarterly against realized calibration; if the slope drifts toward 1.0 (market becomes efficient), reduce extremizing.
- **Immediately exclude** short-horizon crypto price markets, ambiguous-resolution novelty markets, and near-resolution 95¢+ favorites from the forecasting universe.

## Caveats
- **Single-data-point risk in politics:** the 2024 election is one observation; as Rajiv Sethi notes, markets did *not* correctly forecast several other outcomes, and "the jury is still out on markets vs models." Do not overfit strategy to 2024.
- **Documented biases are small in magnitude.** The favorite-longshot reversal averages under a cent per decile; the exploitable content is in *systematic recalibration across many markets*, not any single trade.
- **Read-only vs tradable edge differ.** Much of the "edge" literature (arbitrage, negRisk, cross-platform) is about *trading* profit that fees, spread, and settlement risk erode. For a calibration-scoring lab that only needs to beat the *price* on Brier/log-loss, the relevant edges are recalibration and model-drivable categories — these survive even where trading profit would not.
- **Source-quality flags:** several practitioner "edge finder" tools and arbitrage blogs are marketing content with unverified track records (e.g., self-reported signal-service win/loss records) — treated here as illustrative of method, not as evidence. The strongest claims rest on the arXiv/SSRN datasets, ForecastBench/Metaculus published results, and the Good Judgment/GJP literature. Do not confuse third-party "accuracy" figures with the academic papers' own numbers.
- **Dataset-size discrepancy:** Reichenbach & Walther's abstract was revised from "124 million" to "478 million" trades between its Dec 2025 and May 2026 versions; the latest figure is 478M.
- **LLM parity is a projection, not a fact.** The "parity by late 2026/2027" figures are linear extrapolations with wide confidence intervals and should be treated as speculative, not established.
