## 1.1 Motivation and the problem

Prediction-market prices have graduated from curiosity to infrastructure. They are quoted by newsrooms, consumed by decision-makers, and — the sharpest recent evidence — taken seriously by professional forecasters' own institutions: contracts on inflation and monetary policy now match or beat consensus surveys and federal-funds futures (Diercks, Katz & Wright, 2026). The economic case built two decades ago (Wolfers & Zitzewitz, 2004; Arrow et al., 2008) has, on this reading, been vindicated by adoption.

Yet the same literature that certifies these prices also documents systematic cracks in them. The most robust is horizon-dependent miscalibration: prices far from resolution are compressed toward one-half, with estimated calibration slopes rising from near one at short horizons to roughly 1.3 beyond a month on 292 million trades (Le, 2026), a pattern first identified on Intrade a decade earlier (Page & Clemen, 2013). And the pattern now has two live, mutually incompatible readings. The behavioral reading takes it at face value: long-horizon beliefs are systematically underconfident, and a simple recalibration should beat the price. The rational reading (Grant, Johnstone & Kwon, 2019; Maresca, 2026; Gebele & Matthes, 2026) says much of the apparent miscalibration is a cost-of-capital discount on collateral locked until settlement — Gebele & Matthes remove 48–88% of the raw horizon gradient by adjusting for it — in which case the "anomaly" is compensation, not error, and recalibrating against it earns nothing. Strikingly, the two camps' flagship papers do not cite one another.

Every study on both sides shares one structural weakness: the hypotheses were formed on the same resolved data that test them. Retrospective designs are where selection, specification search, and publication incentives do their quiet work, and the equity-anomalies literature has measured the consequence — published return predictors decay substantially once the papers documenting them appear (McLean & Pontiff, 2016). Whether a *prediction-market* anomaly survives its own publication has never been asked, because asking it properly requires something this literature has never had: forecasts committed before outcomes, hypotheses committed before forecasts, and inference valid under continuous observation.

## 1.2 What we do

This paper builds that instrument and reports its first confirmatory campaign. Beginning [2026-07-02], a read-only system continuously collected prices, order books, and resolutions from multiple prediction venues, generated probability forecasts from a fixed roster of pre-specified models, and froze every forecast in an append-only ledger whose daily state is hash-committed to a public repository — establishing, without trusting the author, that each forecast existed before the outcome it predicts (§4.2). A pre-analysis plan committed on 2026-07-06, before any confirmatory resolution existed, fixed three primary hypotheses, the sole claim-bearing statistic, exclusion rules, and an analysis freeze of 2026-12-31; the plan's body is frozen, with changes possible only as dated, appended addenda (§4.3).

The three hypotheses map onto the open questions of §1.1. **H1** tests whether Le's horizon-dependent miscalibration persists out of sample, after its publication — and, via a stratification by the settlement-wedge-compressing venue features that the rational reading identifies (negRisk convertibility, collateral-yield programs), committed before any data could speak to it (Addendum 9.3; §4.9), whether what persists is belief error or funding friction. **H2** tests whether pre-specified statistical corrections achieve positive paired skill against the venue's own contemporaneous price in the two categories where external structural models exist (macroeconomic releases, weather), net of simulated trading costs. **H3** tests whether an equal-weight pool of external venues' prices on matched events carries predictive value for the home venue's price. Inference throughout is by time-uniform confidence sequences (Howard et al., 2021; Choe & Ramdas, 2024): the system scores itself nightly, its operator looks, and the intervals are built so that looking is statistically free (§4.5). A continuously running negative control — sports markets, where our models have no plausible edge — guards the harness itself (§4.7).

Two boundary statements up front. This is an efficiency test, not a trading paper: the system is read-only by construction, holds no accounts, places no orders, and its simulated portfolio exists to bound economic significance, not to advertise returns (§4.8). And the pre-registered object is the *system together with its update protocol* — versioned models, walk-forward refits, gated promotions — not a frozen parameter vector; what that means and why it is the honest formulation for a live system is developed in §4.3.

## 1.4 Contributions

Three, in order of novelty. **First, methodological:** to our knowledge the first live, cryptographically pre-registered efficiency test of a prediction market — an ex-ante design in a literature that is, without exception we could find, ex post (§2.6). The instrument (ledger, commitment chain, pre-registration protocol for a self-updating system, anytime-valid monitoring, negative control) is independent of what it measured and is reusable as-is. **Second, empirical:** the first out-of-sample, post-publication persistence test of a named prediction-market anomaly, designed to adjudicate between its behavioral and rational-discount readings on data that existed when neither camp's hypotheses did. **Third, infrastructural:** the full system is open-source with [415] automated tests, a one-command replication export, and a commitment-verification procedure that requires no cooperation from the author (§4.10).
