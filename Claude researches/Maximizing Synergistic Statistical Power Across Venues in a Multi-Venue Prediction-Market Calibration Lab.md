# Maximizing Synergistic Statistical Power Across Venues in a Multi-Venue Prediction-Market Calibration Lab

## TL;DR
- **Partially pool, never fully pool.** A Bayesian (or empirical-Bayes) hierarchical logistic recalibration with venue × category × horizon random effects on slope/intercept is the single highest-leverage change: it borrows strength across the ~327,000 binary contracts you can now access while preserving venue-specific offsets. At the small-n cells that matter (a new venue's first month), partial pooling substantially reduces calibration-parameter variance versus per-cell fitting, and the documented structure — universal horizon underconfidence (slope rising from ~0.99 near resolution to ~1.32 beyond a month) plus a large politics bias — is real and cross-venue, so pooling the *shape* while keeping venue intercepts is exactly right.
- **Cluster by event, aggregate in log-odds with correlation-aware extremization, and measure skill with anytime-valid methods.** Because Kalshi/Polymarket/Metaculus/Manifold often price the *same* underlying event, treat the event as the bootstrap cluster (not the venue-market), aggregate venues via a log-odds (logarithmic) pool with an extremization factor bounded well below the independent-source ideal (correlation discounts effective N), and monitor skill with Choe–Ramdas confidence sequences / e-processes so your nightly report never suffers peeking bias.
- **The biggest realistic power win is not more venues — it is variance reduction.** Paired scoring against the market as a control variate, event-level clustering, stratification by category/horizon, and CLV-style intermediate outcomes (continuous, ~10× lower SD than binary win/loss) will detect a given edge in a fraction of the samples. For a solo Python codebase, implement empirical-Bayes / ridge-penalized hierarchical GLMs now and reserve full MCMC for a quarterly offline audit.

## Key Findings

### 1. Hierarchical / partial-pooling recalibration is the core engine
The prediction-market-specific evidence is unusually strong and recent. Le (2026), *Decomposing Crowd Wisdom* (arXiv 2602.19520), used **292 million trades across 327,000 binary contracts on Kalshi and Polymarket** and showed calibration is a *structured, multidimensional* phenomenon that a hierarchical model captures well (96.3% posterior predictive coverage). The decomposition:
- **Universal horizon effect** (30.2% of calibration variance): all domains drift toward underconfidence at long horizons — mean logit-calibration slope rises from **0.99 within one hour of resolution to 1.32 beyond one month**. A contract at 70¢ one month out corresponds to a true probability nearer 75%. Holds on *both* Kalshi and Polymarket.
- **Domain structural bias** (14.6%): politics is a persistent underconfidence outlier (intercept +0.15); weather and entertainment are mildly overconfident (−0.09). The politics bias replicates across venues (Kalshi mean slope 1.64, Polymarket 1.31).
- **Domain × horizon interaction** (26.0%): the single largest domain-specific term — sports well-calibrated short-term (slopes 0.90–1.10) but underconfident beyond a month (1.74); weather overconfident short-term.
- **Trade-size scale effect** (16.5%): larger trades → more compressed politics prices *on Kalshi* (Δ=0.53, 95% CI [0.29,0.75]) but **does not replicate on Polymarket** (Δ=0.11, [−0.15,0.39]) — a warning that microstructure effects are venue-specific and must not be pooled.

**Implication for your M1/M1.2/… design:** the *slope-vs-horizon curve* and the *politics underconfidence* are shared structure worth pooling; the *trade-size effect and intercepts* are venue-idiosyncratic and must stay per-venue. This is precisely the partial-pooling compromise: shared hyperpriors on the horizon-slope function, venue- and category-specific random intercepts/slopes shrunk toward the global mean in proportion to their cell's noise.

**How much power partial pooling buys.** The classic result (Gelman/Efron–Morris James-Stein lineage) is that shrinkage strictly dominates unpooled MLE in multi-parameter estimation, and the gain is largest exactly where n is small. For a venue-category-horizon cell with few resolved markets, the partial-pooling estimate is a precision-weighted average of the cell estimate and the global mean; the effective variance reduction is roughly a factor of (1 + n_cell/n_shrinkage). At the small-n regime of a newly added venue (Metaculus resolves only ~40 questions/month), this can cut the standard error of the recalibration slope substantially versus fitting that cell alone, at the cost of a small, self-limiting bias. Bias is negligible provided variance components are well estimated (the main risk is *underestimating* between-cell variance, which over-shrinks).

**Empirical Bayes for category base rates (M2).** Use the Beta-Binomial conjugate model with method-of-moments hyperparameters: fit a Beta(α₀,β₀) to the distribution of per-category resolution rates, then the shrunk base rate for category i is p_i = (s_i + α₀)/(s_i + f_i + α₀ + β₀) — i.e., add α₀ "phantom yes" and β₀ "phantom no." Method-of-moments closed forms: α₀ = μ(μ(1−μ)/σ² − 1), β₀ = (1−μ)/μ · α₀, where μ, σ² are the mean and variance of observed category rates. This is milliseconds to compute, needs no MCMC, and with hundreds of categories the EB plug-in is essentially identical to the fully Bayesian posterior mean.

**Multi-task/transfer framing.** The venue-as-group structure *is* the transfer-learning solution: shared global hyperpriors = the "shared representation," venue random effects = task-specific heads. There is no substantial published literature on *cross-market* calibration transfer in prediction markets specifically beyond Le (2026) and the recalibration papers it cites (Liu et al. Bayesian boldness-recalibration; Satopää et al. logit combination); this is a genuine gap you would be contributing to, which argues for keeping the design defensible and simple.

### 2. Aggregation theory — combine in log-odds, discount for correlation, extremize modestly
- **Linear vs log vs extremized.** A plain weighted average (linear opinion pool) is provably sub-optimal and systematically *underconfident* when sources draw on overlapping information — it must be extremized (Satopää et al. 2014; Baron et al. 2014 "Two reasons to make aggregated forecasts more extreme"; Neyman–Roughgarden 2022). The **logarithmic opinion pool** (average the logits, i.e., your existing M3/M4 log-odds machinery) is the natural aggregator: it satisfies external Bayesianity and is the appropriate form when sources have *distinct* information sets.
- **How much to extremize under correlation.** Satopää et al. empirically recommend a logit scaling factor of ~2. Neyman & Roughgarden, "Are You Smarter Than a Random Expert? The Robust Aggregation of Substitutable Signals" (2022, 23rd ACM EC, pp. 990–1012), give a principled, worst-case-optimal factor d = n(√(3n²−3n+1)−2)/(n²−n−1) → √3 ≈ 1.73 as n→∞, valid under a projective-substitutes condition on experts' signals. But cross-venue forecasts of the same event are highly correlated (they watch each other — see lead-lag below), so the effective number of independent sources is far below the raw count. **Discount effective sample size before extremizing:** estimate pairwise venue correlation from historical co-priced events and compute an effective N via the standard poll-aggregation adjustment (Cov(X_A,X_B) = ½[Var(X_A)+Var(X_B)−Var(X_A−X_B)]); over-extremizing correlated sources is the classic overconfidence failure mode.
- **Weighting.** Weight venues by historical category-specific skill (log-loss vs market), liquidity/volume (thin Manifold markets are noisier), and recency (time-decay). Fit the per-category log-odds weights with regularization (elastic net / ridge) as Atanasov et al. did — regularization matters more than the exact aggregator at your sample sizes.
- **Disagreement as information.** Cross-venue spread is a usable uncertainty signal: forecast-combination literature (Knüppel–Krüger) shows disagreement correlates with squared error of the combined forecast under some conditions. Concretely, log the cross-venue log-odds standard deviation as a feature and test whether it predicts |error| — if it does, it both flags low-confidence forecasts and can widen your intervals.
- **Do markets and Metaculus differ in calibration?** Yes, and it is category-dependent. Metaculus community/Metaculus prediction Brier ≈ 0.107–0.111 on its resolved set (best of any public platform on its own questions), but this is on a *different, harder-to-compare* question pool. The GJP experimental result — Atanasov et al., "Distilling the Wisdom of Crowds: Prediction Markets vs. Prediction Polls," *Management Science* 63(3):691–706 (2017): "More than 2,400 participants made forecasts on 261 events over two seasons"; "In both seasons... prices from the prediction market were more accurate than the simple mean of forecasts from prediction polls" — found that performance-weighted/recalibrated team polls in turn outperformed markets, and crowd *skill* mattered more than the elicitation mechanism. Play-money Manifold (Brier ≈ 0.168) is meaningfully behind real-money venues but far better than chance, and within a few points of real-money markets on overlapping questions. Takeaway: keep per-venue recalibration; do not assume Metaculus is universally better — it depends on category and horizon.

### 3. Statistical power mechanics for skill detection
- **Paired designs.** Score your model vs the market at the *same timestamp on the same market* and analyze the paired difference. Paired Brier/log-loss differences remove the enormous between-market variance (question difficulty), which is the dominant variance component — this is your single biggest classical power lever and you already do it.
- **Event-level clustering is mandatory.** When Kalshi, Polymarket, Manifold, and Metaculus all price "Will X win the election?", those are **not independent observations**. Cluster the bootstrap by *underlying event*, not by venue-market. Naive row-level bootstrap understates uncertainty (documented repeatedly for nested data); cluster bootstrap that resamples whole event-clusters restores nominal coverage. Caveat: cluster bootstrap needs a reasonably large *number of clusters* (rule of thumb ~≥ a few dozen) to be well-behaved; with few events, prefer cluster-robust variance or a hierarchical model.
- **MDE with clustering.** The design effect inflates variance by ≈ 1 + (m̄−1)·ICC, where m̄ is average cluster size and ICC the intra-cluster correlation of the score difference. Report MDE and the "wasted N" from clustering explicitly — adding a fourth venue for an event already priced on three adds far less than one independent observation.
- **Anytime-valid sequential testing.** Your nightly-refreshed ledger creates a peeking problem: fixed-sample p-values are invalid under continuous monitoring. Use **confidence sequences / e-processes** (Howard–Ramdas; Waudby-Smith–Ramdas betting CS). The directly on-point method is **Choe & Ramdas, "Comparing Sequential Forecasters," *Operations Research* 72(4):1368–1387 (2024; online Oct 17 2023), code at github.com/yjchoe/ComparingForecasters** — distribution-free, variance-adaptive confidence sequences for the *time-varying mean score difference between two forecasters* that "correctly cover the score difference under continuous monitoring... anytime valid," plus e-processes for the weak null "model is no better than market on average." This is exactly your model-vs-market comparison and is safe under arbitrary optional stopping — the correct backbone for a monthly-reviewed ledger. E-values also combine cleanly by multiplication across categories/filtrations.
- **Stratification.** Stratify by category and horizon (you already bucket horizon). Stratified estimation with variance pooled within strata increases power when the effect and variance differ by stratum, which Le (2026) proves they do.

### 4. Variance reduction and sharper scoring
- **Control variates using the market price.** This is the formal version of paired scoring and worth implementing explicitly. For a target score Y (your model's Brier) and control X (market's Brier) with known/estimable mean, the adjusted estimator Ỹ = Y − θ(X − X̄) has variance Var(Y)(1 − ρ²), where ρ = corr(model score, market score) and optimal θ = Cov(Y,X)/Var(X). Because model and market scores are highly correlated on the same markets (ρ often 0.8–0.95), this cuts variance by 1−ρ² — potentially a **4–10× effective-sample gain** for the *difference* estimate. Estimate θ by regression; it is the same math as CUPED in A/B testing.
- **CRPS / proper scoring for non-binary markets.** CRPS = ∫(F(x) − 1{x≥y})² dx generalizes Brier to numeric/multi-outcome markets and uses the whole predictive CDF, so it extracts more information per resolution than a binary indicator. Adding Kalshi/Metaculus numeric and range markets and scoring with CRPS genuinely adds power (more informative observations), and CRPS decomposes into calibration + sharpness. Metaculus's own analysis found CRPS and log-score can rank the *same* forecasts differently, so report both.
- **CLV as a high-power leading indicator.** In sports betting, closing-line value is the professional skill proxy *because it has ~10× lower variance than realized win/loss*: per Joseph Buchdahl (Pinnacle, "Closing Line Value demystified"), "the typical standard deviation in even-money profits and losses will be about 1.00," while CLV varies in far smaller increments (≈0.1). The practical consequence is dramatic: per Buchdahl, a 5%-edge bettor "might only need 50 bets to demonstrate significance" via CLV, versus "several thousand bets" to rule out chance using realized profit alone. Your read-only analogue: measure whether the market price *drifts toward your model's view* between forecast-freeze and resolution (price-drift / "closing-price value"). This is a continuous, fast, pre-resolution outcome that multiplies your effective sample size and gives an early-warning signal months before enough markets resolve. Validity caveat: CLV only proxies skill if the closing price is efficient; document this and still confirm with resolution-based scoring.

### 5. What else strengthens the edge (noted, not deep-dived)
- **Cross-venue lead-lag as features.** Ng et al. (2026, cited in arXiv 2604.24147) found **Polymarket leads Kalshi in price discovery** during the 2024 election (Polymarket prices predict future Kalshi prices, not vice versa), with persistent divergences above the ~2% arbitrage bound. A read-only lab can use the leading venue's move as a feature to nowcast the lagging venue's short-horizon drift. Also relevant: persistent violations of the law of one price across venues (arXiv 2601.01706) due to differing resolution semantics.
- **Order-book microstructure.** Imbalance and depth-asymmetry features from public Kalshi/Polymarket order books are candidate short-horizon drift predictors; standard microstructure results support this, though prediction-market-specific validation is thin.
- **Smart-money / on-chain wallet tracking.** Polymarket trades are on-chain; tracking historically-profitable wallets is a documented (if noisy) public-data signal.
- **Seasonality / attention effects.** Time-of-day and event-driven attention spikes affect liquidity and mispricing windows; usable as regime features. Clean volume-based features before trusting them: Sirolly, Ma, Kanoria & Sethi, "Network-Based Detection of Wash Trading" (Columbia Business School Research Paper No. 5714122, Nov 6 2025), estimate ~25% of Polymarket historical volume was wash trading, "peaking at nearly 60 percent of volume in December 2024," and concentrated in sports (45% of all-time sports-market volume, vs 17% elections, 12% politics, 3% crypto; ~$4.5B total, 14% of 1.26M wallets flagged).

### 6. Practical implementation guidance
**Complexity tier — pick the middle tier now.** For a solo-maintained polars/numpy/scipy + SQLite/Parquet stack:
- **Do now (empirical Bayes + penalized GLM):** Fit hierarchical logistic recalibration as a **ridge-penalized logistic regression** with venue×category×horizon dummies, where the ridge penalty *is* the partial-pooling shrinkage (ridge = Gaussian random effects with fixed variance). Use scikit-learn/statsmodels or a short numpy/scipy IRLS. Beta-Binomial EB for base rates in closed form. This gives ~90% of the benefit at ~10% of the complexity and no sampler.
- **Quarterly offline audit (full Bayes):** Fit the full hierarchical model in **NumPyro** (fast, JAX-based, good for a monthly/quarterly batch) or **cmdstanpy/PyMC** to get honest variance components and check that your ridge penalties (which fix the shrinkage strength) aren't over/under-shrinking. This validates the cheap estimator; you don't need it nightly.
- **Skill measurement:** Port Choe–Ramdas confidence sequences (their repo is small, pure Python) for the nightly model-vs-market comparison; cluster-bootstrap by event for the periodic point estimates. Keep the sports null control as a negative control.
- **Pitfalls to code around:** (a) the ridge/EB shrinkage strength must be tuned by marginal likelihood or CV, not guessed; (b) confidence sequences are wider than fixed-n CIs — that is the correct price of anytime validity, don't "fix" it; (c) don't extremize before discounting correlation.

**Sample-size reality (resolved binary markets, from Le 2026 and platform data).** The dataset you're tapping is large but *lopsided*:
- **Kalshi: 210,608 binary contracts through 31 Dec 2025, 98.6% of past-close contracts resolved** — implying on the order of ~15,000 resolved/month in 2025, but heavily sports (sports markets only launched Jan 2025 and dominate volume; ~66.7% of Kalshi trades). Politics is only ~6,609 markets (3.1%).
- **Polymarket: ~116,000 resolved binary contracts** through end-2025 (Le 2026); the HuggingFace trade dataset spans 268,000+ markets (all statuses); on-chain archives balloon to ~1.3M "markets" once auto-generated 5-minute crypto markets are included — do **not** treat those as independent samples.
- **Manifold: >130,000 markets created** (binary + multiple-choice + numeric), low-thousands created/month, but many are personal/joke/N-A markets — filter aggressively.
- **Metaculus: ~4,000+ resolved of >8,600 submitted (~40/month, ~40–45% binary)** — small-throughput, long-horizon, the clearest beneficiary of partial pooling.

**What 3–6 months realistically buys vs Polymarket-only.** In categories where venues overlap (politics, crypto, macro, some sports), you are mostly adding *correlated* observations — event-level clustering means the marginal independent information is modest, maybe a 1.3–1.8× effective-N gain rather than 4×. The larger, more independent gains come from: (i) **variance reduction** (control variates + CLV-style drift) which can deliver multiples of effective N on the *same* markets immediately; (ii) **category breadth** — Metaculus/Manifold reach science/tech/long-horizon questions Polymarket underserves, improving your hierarchical model's coverage and base rates; and (iii) **cross-venue disagreement features**. Net: expect meaningfully faster skill detection within 3–6 months primarily from variance reduction and event-clustered pooling, not from raw sample multiplication.

**Pitfalls specific to multi-venue archives.**
- **Question-matching errors** contaminate event clusters: wrongly merging two differently-resolving contracts (different resolution semantics — the law-of-one-price-violation papers show this is common across Kalshi/Polymarket) either fabricates fake "disagreement" or fake "agreement." Match conservatively; when unsure, keep markets in separate clusters (costs power but preserves validity).
- **Survivorship/selection in archives.** GJP/Good Judgment Open and any resolved-PredictIt data are selected samples (which questions were asked, which resolved, which platform survived). Don't treat them as random draws from the same population as live markets; use them for structure/priors, not headline skill claims.
- **Manifold play-money reliability.** Documented calibration studies (Manifold's own calibration page; wasabipesto's "Calibration City"; the Manifund platform-comparison project) show aggregate calibration within a few points of real-money markets, but thinly-traded markets are noisy and sweepcash was sunset March 2025 — weight Manifold by market liquidity and treat lightly-traded markets as high-variance.
- **Metaculus community-prediction hiding window.** The Community Prediction is hidden for an initial window on each question (to avoid anchoring), and the Metaculus Prediction was deprecated Nov 2024 — so early-in-life snapshots may lack an available aggregate. Handle missing-aggregate windows explicitly in your snapshotter.

## Recommendations
1. **Immediately (weeks):** Implement the market-price **control variate** on your existing paired Brier/log-loss (biggest instant power gain), and switch periodic CIs to **event-level cluster bootstrap**. Add **CLV-style price-drift** as a continuous secondary outcome.
2. **Near-term (1–2 months):** Replace naive per-venue recalibration with a **ridge-penalized hierarchical logistic GLM** (venue×category×horizon), tuning shrinkage by CV/marginal likelihood; add **Beta-Binomial EB** base rates. Keep venue intercepts and trade-size terms strictly per-venue.
3. **Near-term:** Adopt **Choe–Ramdas confidence sequences / e-processes** for the nightly model-vs-market skill readout to eliminate peeking bias; report e-values that multiply across categories.
4. **Aggregation:** Use a **log-odds pool with correlation-discounted extremization** — estimate venue correlations from co-priced events, compute effective N, and cap extremization below the √3 / factor-2 independent-source level. Fit per-category weights with elastic net.
5. **Quarterly:** Run the **full Bayesian hierarchical model** (NumPyro/PyMC) as an audit to validate shrinkage strengths and variance components.
6. **Benchmarks that change the plan:** If between-cell variance components come back near zero (venues/categories behave alike) → collapse toward more pooling and fewer per-venue models. If cross-venue correlation is very high (>0.9 on co-priced events) → stop counting extra venues as added N and lean entirely on variance-reduction + breadth. If CLV-drift fails to predict resolution error in your sports null control → don't trust it as a skill proxy in other categories.

## Caveats
- **Le (2026) is a preprint** (arXiv 2602.19520, Feb 2026) and its trade-size scale effect already fails to replicate across venues — treat its *magnitudes* as indicative, not settled, though the qualitative horizon and politics patterns are corroborated by older favourite-longshot-bias literature.
- **Platform Brier numbers are not directly comparable** across venues (different question pools, difficulty, horizons); the 0.107/0.111/0.168 figures describe each platform on its own questions, not head-to-head.
- **Anytime-valid methods trade width for safety:** confidence sequences are wider than fixed-n intervals at any single look; the payoff is validity under continuous monitoring, which your nightly report needs.
- **Cluster bootstrap needs enough clusters;** with few overlapping events in a category, prefer hierarchical-model or cluster-robust variance over the bootstrap.
- Some cited figures come from **commercial review/aggregator sites** (platform Brier scores, volume trackers) rather than peer-reviewed sources; the academic backbone (Satopää, Neyman–Roughgarden, Choe–Ramdas, Howard–Ramdas, Atanasov et al., Le 2026, Efron/Morris EB) is solid, but treat venue-level descriptive statistics as approximate.
