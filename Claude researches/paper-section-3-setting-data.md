# 3. Institutional Setting and Data

Numbers in [brackets] are placeholders or facts to re-verify at the analysis freeze — venue rules changed during the sample period and may change again before it closes. This section describes the venues, their settlement mechanics, our data collection, and the rules that define which markets enter the analysis. We give the institutional details more space than is customary because three of them — settlement timing, resolution disputes, and wash trading — are not background color but direct threats to inference that the design of §4 answers, and the reader should be able to check that the answers match the institutions as they actually work.

## 3.1 Venues

The system observes four venues spanning three distinct incentive structures, recorded internally as trust tiers: *money* (Polymarket, Kalshi), *reputation* (Metaculus), and *play* (Manifold). The tier determines what a venue's data is allowed to do in this study — a rule fixed in the specification, not an analysis-time choice.

**Polymarket** is a blockchain-settled central-limit-order-book exchange: binary outcome tokens collateralized in a dollar-pegged stablecoin trade on [Polygon], with each market carrying a YES and a NO token whose prices sum to approximately one. Markets are created with verbatim resolution criteria; a subset are grouped into *negRisk* sets — mutually exclusive, jointly exhaustive outcome families (e.g., "who wins the nomination") whose YES prices must sum to one and whose positions are convertible against each other, an institutional feature that matters twice below (§3.2, §5.5). Jurisdictional access rules changed during our sample period [verify status at writing: US re-entry via a regulated derivatives entity; access restrictions elsewhere]; none of this affects our design, because the system never holds an account or accepts terms of service on any venue — it reads the same public market-data endpoints available to any researcher (§3.3). Recent academic archives give a sense of scale, and are worth distinguishing precisely since more than one now exists at similar magnitude: Qin & Yang (2026) release a 1.2-billion-trade, 1.3-million-market ground-truth-direction archive (the *Polymarket-v1 Database*) spanning Polymarket's first-generation contract from inception to its 2026-04-28 retirement — a different, independent archive from the one this paper's own bootstrap uses (§3.4) — while Le (2026) analyzes 292 million trades for the calibration finding central to H1.

**Kalshi** is a CFTC-regulated designated contract market offering cash-settled binary event contracts, with particularly deep and frequently-resolving coverage of scheduled economic releases and weather — the categories our P1/P2 priorities target (§3.5). Its regulatory position and settlement mechanics differ from Polymarket's in ways that are useful rather than inconvenient for us: two venues with different trader populations, collateral regimes, and resolution authorities pricing the same real-world events are closer to independent measurements than two crypto venues would be. Institutional evidence on the quality of its macro contracts is discussed with model M5 (§5.4).

**Metaculus** is not a market: it is a reputation-scored forecasting platform whose community prediction aggregates individual probability forecasts. It carries no prices, spreads, or capital at risk. We collect its community prediction as a signal (input to M7, §5.6) and as resolution data, but never treat it as a forecast target — there is no tradable price to beat — and when a community prediction is hidden by tournament rules, we record the absence rather than impute a value.

**Manifold** is a play-money exchange. Play-money incentives are different in kind, not merely in degree, so Manifold data is excluded from every skill claim, every model fit, and the cross-venue pool; it contributes only to event mapping and category base rates, provenance-tagged (§4.6). We collect it at all because its low listing friction makes it a useful index of *which events exist*.

The same real-world event frequently trades on more than one venue. This overlap is an asset (independent crowds; the M7 signal) and a hazard (pseudo-replication in inference; a mismatched pair poisons two analyses at once), and both edges are handled where they cut: conservative human-confirmed matching in §4.4 and §5.6, event-level clustering in §4.4.

## 3.2 Resolution and settlement mechanics

Polymarket markets resolve through UMA's optimistic oracle: a proposed outcome stands unless disputed within a window on the order of [two hours], with disputes escalating to a token-holder vote. Two consequences for measurement follow. First, the *first reported* outcome is not the outcome: our resolution watcher records only final payouts, and every market that passed through a formal dispute carries a persistent flag; PAP Addendum 9.2(b) commits that all primary analyses will be re-run excluding disputed markets, a pass not yet run at the time of this writing (§4.9). Second, resolution wording is a real failure surface — outcomes can hinge on phrasing — but we do not claim to detect this automatically: no ambiguity classifier exists, by deliberate design rather than by omission, because building one would itself be exactly the kind of editorial judgment the no-selection rule (§4.6) forbids — a model that decides which questions are "too ambiguous to trust" is silently choosing its own easy cases. The only path by which a market is excluded on these grounds is a manual, human-invoked command that logs the exclusion (reason code `manual`) for the record; it does not block that market from being forecast unless a human has separately acted on it, and no automated rule stands between a poorly-worded question and the forecast universe. Kalshi contracts settle by exchange determination against a named source; the same final-outcome and flagging discipline applies.

Settlement timing deserves its own paragraph, because it is the institutional fact behind the strongest rival explanation our H1 faces. A prediction-market position ties up collateral until settlement, and settlement can lag resolution; a near-certain contract with months to run is therefore, mechanically, also a fixed-income instrument — its price embeds a discount for locked capital, not only a probability (Grant, Johnstone & Kwon, 2019; Gebele & Matthes, 2026). Two venue features compress this wedge differentially, and we record both: negRisk convertibility on Polymarket (capital in one leg of an exhaustive set is not fully locked, since positions convert) and collateral-yield programs — Kalshi pays interest on balances [terms as of writing; verify], and Polymarket introduced holding rewards on eligible markets [scope and rate; verify] — which pay the waiting cost the discount would otherwise price. These features are exactly the stratification variables of the pre-registered settlement-discounting robustness check (PAP Addendum 9.3; §4.9): they exist at the venue and market level, they were not designed by us, and they vary within our sample, which is what makes the stratification informative.

## 3.3 Data collection

All data comes from public, unauthenticated endpoints — market metadata, order books, price histories, and resolution states on the exchanges; the community prediction and question metadata on Metaculus — under conservative self-imposed rate limits well below documented ceilings, with exponential backoff and a global kill switch. The system holds no account anywhere, accepts no terms of service, and places no orders; its collection is the same act as an academic downloading public market data, performed continuously.

Our own collection began 2026-07-02 for Polymarket and 2026-07-04 for Kalshi and Manifold; the confirmatory window of the pre-analysis plan opened 2026-07-06 (§4.2–4.3). Metaculus collection has not begun at all, for a reason worth stating precisely rather than glossing as a simple delay: live verification found that Metaculus's anonymous API access is gone (a confirmed 403 response, not a transient block), and even with an operator-supplied API token, the specific aggregated-prediction field this system needs returns null for real, heavily-forecasted questions — that field appears to sit behind a separate commercial data-access tier the specification's no-account-creation rule (§4.6) does not authorize us to acquire. The Metaculus collector and client are implemented and will activate the moment a token with the right access tier is available; nothing about §5.6's M7 design depends on Metaculus specifically, since M7's confirmed pairs are Kalshi-only at present in any case (§5.6). The collector snapshots order books and mid-prices at a base cadence of [5] minutes for the liquid tier and 60 minutes for the tail, with top-of-book and [10]-level depth stored alongside price; for human-confirmed cross-venue event pairs, cadence tightens to [1] minute on both legs, because the lead–lag question (H3) is underpowered on a coarse grid and fine-grained history cannot be recaptured later. Snapshots land in append-only columnar storage partitioned by date; state (markets, resolutions, forecasts, versions) lives in a single write-ahead-logged SQLite database. A resolution watcher polls closed markets until final payout; a status subsystem tracks per-venue freshness and gaps. Collection gaps happen — hosts restart, networks fail — and the design treats them as data rather than as something to paper over: gaps are recorded, and one specific downstream metric that is directly sensitive to them — the closing-line-value drift diagnostic of §4.7 — excludes and counts windows that overlap a recorded gap. We note the scope of this precisely rather than imply a general guarantee: this gap-awareness is CLV-specific at present; Brier, log loss, and RPS are computed directly from ledger rows and do not carry separate gap-exclusion logic of their own, so a gap's effect on those metrics is bounded only by however few forecasts a genuine outage prevents from ever being written, not by an explicit downstream filter.

One further collection detail matters for interpretation regardless of venue: market metadata can exist for markets with no live order book, so universe eligibility filters on order-book activity and liquidity fields, not on metadata presence alone. (The tournament-hidden-prediction handling described for Metaculus in §3.1 — record the absence, never impute — is a property of the collector's design, ready for whenever collection actually begins; it has not yet had occasion to apply.)

## 3.4 Historical data and its limits

Model fitting needs resolved history that predates our own collection. The primary source is a public research archive of Polymarket's trade history through the end of 2025 — 1.1 billion trade records across 268,706 markets — filtered to resolved binary markets with prices observable at multiple horizons. We name this archive carefully rather than attach a convenient citation to it: it carries no published-paper attribution in our own specification, and it is a *different* archive from the one described in §3.1 — Qin & Yang's (2026) 1.2-billion-trade, 1.3-million-market ground-truth-direction database is a distinct, independently-hosted dataset, and the two should not be conflated despite similar scale and subject matter; an earlier internal draft of our own reading notes made exactly this conflation, which is how we know to warn against it here. Its role in this system is strictly bounded regardless of provenance: it trains pre-window model parameters (M1's and M1.x's curves, M2's base rates) and it never contributes a single observation to any confirmatory skill claim, which are computed exclusively on forecasts our own system froze and committed (§4.2–4.3). One property of this archive has a consequence flagged honestly where it bites: it carries no venue field — it is Polymarket-only by construction — so the hierarchical recalibration layer, whose machinery is general across venues, is at present *trained* on one venue and only *validated* on the others (§5.2). Other historical archives sometimes used in this literature [GJP, PredictIt] are permitted by the specification for base rates only, provenance-tagged, but are not currently wired in; survivorship and selection in such archives make them unusable for anything stronger.

## 3.5 The forecastable universe

The unit of analysis is the binary market. Universe eligibility is rule-based and logged: every market the system considers and excludes is written to an exclusion log with a machine-readable reason code and date, so the analyzed sample is reconstructible from the data rather than from the paper's description of it (§4.6) — the direct answer to "why isn't market X in your sample."

Four category priorities, fixed before the window, define where forecasting effort concentrates: (P1) scheduled economic releases and central-bank decisions, where external structural models exist; (P2) weather threshold markets, likewise; (P3) long-horizon politics and geopolitics — long-horizon here describes the typical member of the category and the mechanism P3 targets (§5.2), not an enforced numeric gate: category membership is assigned from venue tags alone, so a two-day political market is as much a P3 market as a three-hundred-day one, and the horizon effect's own bucket structure (§5.2), not category membership, is what actually discriminates by time-to-resolution; (P4) entertainment and awards, where base rates carry information. Three exclusions are categorical, each with a stated mechanism rather than a taste: all cryptocurrency and equity *price-target* markets at any horizon, because the underlying is close to a martingale and the market price effectively *is* the conditional forecast — there is no model-drivable gap to exploit, only noise to overfit; novelty markets of the "will X say Y" type and any market whose resolution wording is ambiguous, handled as described in §3.2 (a manual, human-invoked exclusion path, not an automated filter); and markets already priced beyond [0.95 / 0.05] as *forecast targets*, because residual mispricing there is dominated by oracle and dispute tail risk rather than by probability error — they remain in calibration statistics, where they carry information, but are never bet-on analogues. Finally, a random sample of sports markets is deliberately retained and forecast by the cheap models as the null control of §4.7: sports prices are among the most efficient available, and measured "skill" there indicts the harness, not the market. One model's coverage is not gated by this sampling design and is worth flagging precisely: M6 (§5.5) scans every active negRisk event set regardless of category, so it can in principle emit forecasts on sports negRisk markets outside the deliberately small null-control sample — it never invokes the LLM and so never breaches the "cheap models only" boundary that coverage-by-structure exists to enforce, but a reader interpreting the null control as "the only sports markets in the ledger" should read M6's coverage as a documented exception to that framing, not an oversight.

Category labels themselves are an observation-quality problem: venue-native tags drift and disagree across venues. All per-category fitting and reporting therefore keys on an internal, versioned category taxonomy mapped from venue tags, with remaps logged — preventing the silent population mixing that a renamed venue tag would otherwise cause.

## 3.6 Liquidity, volume, and trading costs

A meaningful share of reported volume on decentralized prediction markets is wash trading — self-dealing that inflates activity metrics without carrying information — concentrated, in the period studied, in sports markets (Sirolly et al., 2025). We therefore treat *order-book depth*, not volume, as the liquidity measure: tier assignment and liquidity covariates are computed from recorded book depth, while volume is retained in the data as a covariate explicitly documented as contaminated. This choice was made in the specification, not after inspecting results, and it interacts with the null control (§3.5): the category where volume is least trustworthy is the category we use to test the harness, which is the right way around.

Trading costs changed during the sample in a way we can partly date and partly cannot, and we are precise about which is which rather than implying uniform knowledge. Both venues currently charge a probability-dependent taker fee, and the net-of-cost accounting of §4.8 consumes the exact current rates from a *versioned, sourced* fee schedule (`data/fee_schedule.yaml`, sourced 2026-07-09 from each venue's own fee documentation). Kalshi's effective date (2026-07-07) is stated on its own fee page and is treated as known; Polymarket's own documentation states its current fee structure but not when it took effect, so our schedule records an explicit sentinel there rather than a guessed date — we do not claim Polymarket was previously fee-free, only that we do not know the structure's start date from the source we used. Independently, Qin & Yang (2026, §3.1) document a staggered *platform-wide* fee reform via a difference-in-differences design on their own archive, dating category-level activation months directly — crypto in January 2026, sports in February 2026, politics and remaining categories in March 2026 — which is a real, sourced answer to the question our own schedule's sentinel leaves open, though we have not yet incorporated it into `data/fee_schedule.yaml` and flag doing so as a concrete improvement worth making before the freeze rather than a claim this paper currently relies on. Kalshi charges a published taker-fee formula that likewise peaks at even odds [formula and any changes; verify at freeze]. Spread and depth at forecast time are stored in the ledger row itself (§4.2), so effective, market-specific cost estimates need no ex-post reconstruction.
