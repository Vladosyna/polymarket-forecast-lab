Four literatures converge on this paper: the economics of prediction markets as information aggregators; the empirical record of their calibration distortions, together with a recently articulated rational rival to the behavioral reading of those distortions; the theory and microstructure of forecast aggregation across venues; and the methodology of evaluating forecasters honestly under sequential observation. We review each with a deliberate emphasis on what it leaves open, because the paper's contribution is located precisely in those gaps: every empirical study below is retrospective, and none commits its hypotheses before the data that test them exist.

## 2.1 Prediction markets as measurement instruments

The modern economic case for prediction markets — prices as incentive-compatible aggregators of dispersed information — was framed by Wolfers & Zitzewitz (2004) and given an institutional manifesto by Arrow et al. (2008); long-run field evidence from the Iowa markets (Berg, Nelson & Rietz, 2008) and controlled comparisons against structured polling (Atanasov et al., 2017) broadly support the claim that market prices are hard to beat as probability forecasts. Two decades on, the claim has sharpened from "hard to beat" to "beats the professionals": Diercks, Katz & Wright (2026) show that Kalshi's macroeconomic contracts match or outperform Bloomberg consensus surveys and federal-funds futures on CPI, FOMC, and employment outcomes. We read this literature not as a rival but as the definition of our task's difficulty: it is exactly because the price is a strong forecast that beating it is informative (§4.1), and exactly because the macro categories are now professionally benchmarked that our P1 hypothesis (H2) faces a high, well-documented baseline (§5.4). What this literature does not ask is whether the price, good as it is, leaves *systematic, pre-specifiable* residual structure on the table — which is where the calibration record comes in.

## 2.2 Calibration distortions and their rational rival

The oldest documented distortion is the favorite–longshot bias, whose mechanism — risk-love versus probability misperception — was disentangled by Snowberg & Wolfers (2010) in betting markets. On prediction markets proper, Page & Clemen (2013) documented what is now the central pattern for this paper: contracts far from expiration are systematically worse calibrated than contracts near it. Le (2026) scales that finding to 292 million trades across Kalshi and Polymarket, estimating calibration slopes that rise from near one at short horizons to roughly 1.3 beyond a month — long-horizon prices are compressed toward one-half — with substantial heterogeneity across domains. The tail behavior, meanwhile, appears era- and venue-specific rather than universal: on the complete lifecycle archive of Polymarket's first-generation exchange, Qin & Yang (2026) document a favorite–longshot *reversal* — longshots overpriced, favorites underpriced — the opposite sign of the classical betting-market pattern, explicitly naming and distinguishing it as such from the pattern Snowberg & Wolfers describe. Trader-level evidence adds a further wrinkle: Reichenbach & Walther (2025) find that fewer than a third of Polymarket traders earn positive profits overall, but that the skilled minority's edge is persistent over time rather than a run of luck. This instability of sign and magnitude at the price level, alongside concentrated and persistent skill at the trader level, is itself a design input for us: our recalibration family fits every bias direction rather than hardcoding any (§5.2).

A recent strand reframes the horizon pattern as rational rather than behavioral. The theoretical ancestor is Grant, Johnstone & Kwon (2019): a prediction-market position locks collateral until settlement, so a contract's price embeds a cost-of-capital discount alongside a probability. Maresca (2026) tests the mechanism directly with an agent-based simulation of LLM traders: the induced pricing bias it finds is itself small (0.72 percentage points, an order of magnitude below prior theoretical and empirical benchmarks), and paying interest on locked positions removes roughly 83% of it — though by the paper's own reading, mostly by tripling market participation (capital at stake rising from 17% to 62% of wealth) rather than by directly correcting the bias, a channel worth keeping distinct from Gebele & Matthes's (2026) empirical version below. That empirical version is the closer analogue to our own design: adjusting near-certain long-horizon contracts for settlement-induced discounting removes between roughly half and nearly all of the raw horizon gradient (48–88% across specifications), with the residual statistically indistinguishable from zero under their most conservative treatment. Gebele & Matthes note the directional similarity to Maresca's estimate themselves while cautioning against reading it as a replication, since the two papers' estimands differ — a caution worth repeating rather than eliding. Notably, Gebele & Matthes and Le analyze the same underlying phenomenon without citing one another — two readings of one pattern, developed independently, with no study yet adjudicating between them on data neither has seen. That adjudication is what our pre-registered design supplies: H1 tests the pattern's persistence out of sample, and a stratification by the wedge-compressing venue features their mechanism identifies (negRisk convertibility, collateral-yield programs; §3.2) was committed to the analysis plan before any confirmatory data existed (Addendum 9.3; §4.9), so that whichever reading survives, it survives a test specified in advance by neither camp.

## 2.3 Aggregation, cross-venue structure, and market quality

A parallel literature asks how to combine probability forecasts and what degrades the raw material. Averaged probability forecasts are systematically underconfident, and extremizing the pool recovers much of the loss (Satopää et al., 2014; Baron et al., 2014); Neyman & Roughgarden (2022) give the robust-aggregation account of *how much* to extremize when sources share information — the correlation-discounted logic our ensemble implements (§5.8). Across venues, documented lead–lag structure between Polymarket and Kalshi (Ng, Peng, Tao & Zhou, 2026) implies the lagging price can be improved by the leading one, the mechanism behind our cross-venue signal and H3 (§5.6). At the trader level, the accuracy of these markets appears to rest on remarkably few shoulders: Gomez-Cram, Guo, Jensen & Kung (2026) attribute the bulk of Polymarket's price discovery to a small informed minority, and Della Vedova (2026) finds that trader profits reflect execution skill rather than directional information, with no trader type beating the price-implied accuracy benchmark on direction alone. These are complements, not rivals, to our question: they explain *who moves* the price, while we ask whether the *resulting* price is improvable by public-information models — a market can be moved by a skilled few and still carry a systematic horizon bias. Finally, the measurement hazards this literature documents feed our design directly: wash trading inflates volume metrics, concentrated in sports (Sirolly et al., 2025) — hence depth-based rather than volume-based liquidity measurement (§3.6) — and Qin & Yang (2026) date Polymarket's 2026 fee reform by category via a difference-in-differences design (crypto in January, sports in February, remaining categories in March), a real and useful anchor for the exact date our own fee schedule currently leaves as an honest placeholder (§3.6, §4.8) — though the reform itself predates our live collection and postdates the bootstrap archive's coverage, sitting in the gap between the two rather than inside either, so it is a source we could use to fix a sentinel value, not a claim about conditions during our own sample.

## 2.4 Evaluating forecasters under continuous observation

A live system that scores itself nightly cannot use fixed-sample inference honestly; the statistics literature has recently supplied the right tool. Time-uniform confidence sequences (Howard, Ramdas, McAuliffe & Sekhon, 2021) provide intervals valid at every look time simultaneously, under optional stopping and continuation; betting-based constructions sharpen this for bounded variables (Waudby-Smith & Ramdas, 2024), and the game-theoretic reading of tests as capital processes (Shafer, 2021) supplies the interpretive frame. Closest to us, Choe & Ramdas (2024) develop anytime-valid inference specifically for *comparing sequential forecasters* — the machinery our claim-bearing statistic instantiates (§4.5). These methods are spreading rapidly in sequential experimentation, but, as of our literature scan [2026-07], we find no application of anytime-valid inference to a live market-efficiency test — the combination this paper's design exists to provide.

## 2.5 Machine forecasters and the fate of published anomalies

Two further strands shape what we test and how honestly it can be tested. First, language-model forecasting: Halawi, Zhang, Yueh-Han & Steinhardt (2024) bring LLM systems near crowd-level accuracy on forecasting benchmarks, but every retrospective LLM evaluation faces a contamination problem this literature acknowledges without fully solving — resolved outcomes plausibly sit in the model's training data, making backtests unfalsifiable. Our LLM component is therefore evaluated live-only and carries no primary hypothesis (§5.7); a randomized coverage boundary that would let its marginal contribution be estimated causally rather than correlationally is specified and implemented but not yet enabled, a gap we flag rather than paper over (§4.6, §5.7). We regard the evaluation harness itself, once that gap is closed, as a contribution to this strand in its own right. Second, the fate of published findings: McLean & Pontiff (2016) document that cross-sectional return predictors in equities decay substantially once the papers documenting them appear — publication is a treatment, and the market responds. That question has never been posed to prediction markets. Le (2026) was posted publicly in February 2026 and explicitly frames its calibration estimates as provisional, inviting re-estimation on new data; our confirmatory window opened in July 2026. H1 is thus, by construction, the first out-of-sample persistence test of a named, published prediction-market anomaly — the McLean–Pontiff question transplanted to a new asset class, with the transplant pre-registered (§4.3).

## 2.6 The gap this paper fills

Every empirical study above shares one property: the data preceded the hypotheses. Retrospective designs have produced the entire calibration record, both of its competing explanations, and all existing evidence on cross-venue structure — and retrospective designs are exactly where selection, specification search, and publication incentives do their quiet work. As of our scan, no study of prediction-market efficiency is live, none is pre-registered, none carries a cryptographic commitment device that lets a reader verify what was predicted and when without trusting the author, and none applies anytime-valid inference to the resulting sequence. This paper's contribution is that combination (§4), applied to the sharpest open empirical question the literature above has produced: whether a documented calibration anomaly survives its own publication, net of the rational-discount mechanism proposed to explain it away.
