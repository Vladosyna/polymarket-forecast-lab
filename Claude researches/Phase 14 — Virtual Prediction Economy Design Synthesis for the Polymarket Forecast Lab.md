# Phase 14 — Virtual Prediction Economy: Design Synthesis for the Polymarket Forecast Lab

## TL;DR
- **Build the virtual economy as an interpretation and monitoring layer, not a new controller.** A per-model virtual bankroll is mathematically the *same object* as your existing log-score scoreboard — maximizing log-wealth (Kelly) is provably equivalent to maximizing log-score, with the difference between a bettor's log-score and the market's equal to the realized log bank multiplier (KL divergence). So virtual bankrolls give you a vivid, human-facing skin over metrics you already compute, plus a literal betting interpretation of your existing anytime-valid confidence sequences (a virtual capital process *is* a test martingale/e-process).
- **For competitive weighting, replace or reconcile your per-category log-odds fit with a log-loss Hedge / multiplicative-weights (MWU) update running on ensemble weights ONLY.** With log loss and learning rate η=1 this is exactly Bayesian model averaging with constant regret ≤ ln N against the best single model; sleeping-expert handling (Chernov–Vovk / Freund–Schapire–Singer–Warmuth "specialists") cleanly solves the fact that M5 nowcasts and M7 cross-venue only cover some markets.
- **On cadence: keep monthly batch refits for anything touching model internals, but ADD a weekly MWU update on the ensemble mixture weights with a small learning rate, weight floor/cap, and bounded step.** This is theoretically safe (small-η MWU is provably slow, conservative continuous adaptation). Event-triggered off-schedule refits (BOCPD/CUSUM) are a defensible middle ground. At your scale (dozens of resolutions/week), the honest assessment is that faster cadence yields modest, hard-to-detect accuracy gains — the statistics of distinguishing near-equal models require hundreds to millions of events — so the primary payoff of Phase 14 is interpretability and disciplined model selection, not a step-change in Brier.

## Key Findings

### 1. Kelly wealth growth and log-score are formally dual
The Kelly criterion sizes bets by maximizing the expected logarithm of wealth, which is equivalent to maximizing the long-term geometric growth rate (Kelly 1956; Breiman 1961; Bernoulli 1738 as the utility antecedent). The forecasting-relevant duality is sharper: if a bettor bets optimally (Kelly) against a bookmaker/market, the difference between their log-score and the market's log-score equals the realized log of their bank multiplier, whose expectation is the Kullback–Leibler divergence (MacKay 2025, "Who has the best probabilities? Luck versus skill in prediction tournaments," arXiv:2509.08744: "If the gambler bets optimally ('Kelly betting') then the difference between their log score and that of their bookmaker is the realized value of the log of their bank multiplier, and its expected value is known as the Kullback-Leibler divergence"). Consequently a per-model virtual-bankroll curve and a per-model cumulative-log-score curve are the same information rescaled. The log score itself is the unique local strictly proper scoring rule (Good 1952; Gneiting & Raftery 2007), and it is the unique proper scoring rule whose induced evidence process is a martingale under the null (i.e., the only proper score whose exponentiated cumulative value is a valid, non-decaying e-process).

### 2. Wealth-based ensemble weighting = multiplicative weights = Bayesian model averaging
Cover's universal portfolio (Cover 1991) achieves the growth rate of the best constant-rebalanced portfolio in hindsight, with per-round regret O(m log n)/n → 0; it is the wealth-weighted average of all constant portfolios. Helmbold–Schapire–Singer–Warmuth (1998) showed multiplicative-weights updates approximate the universal portfolio with far less memory. In the "prediction with expert advice" framing (Cesa-Bianchi & Lugosi 2006, *Prediction, Learning, and Games*), the Hedge/exponentially-weighted-average forecaster maintains weights wᵢ ∝ exp(−η·Lᵢ) where Lᵢ is expert i's cumulative loss. Two regimes matter for you:
- **General bounded losses:** the fixed-horizon optimal learning rate is **η = √(8 ln N / T)**, giving regret **≤ √((T/2) ln N)** (Cesa-Bianchi & Lugosi 2006, Theorem 2.2: "with η = √(8 ln N/n), the upper bound becomes √((n/2) ln N)"). The anytime/time-varying variant uses a decaying ηₜ with a slightly different constant (Chernov 2010 improves it to ηₜ = √(4 ln N/t), regret √(n ln N)).
- **Log loss specifically:** with **η = 1, the exponential-weights update reproduces Bayesian model averaging exactly** (Vovk 2001, "Competitive on-line statistics"; restated in Reid–Frongillo–Williamson 2014: "As shown by Vovk [2001], his more general 'aggregating algorithm' reduces to Bayesian updating when log loss is used"). Regret against the best single model is then bounded by **ln N — a constant, with no √T growth** (Vovk's Theorem 1: for an η-mixable loss the aggregating algorithm guarantees cumulative loss ≤ best expert's + log|Θ|/η; log loss is 1-mixable, so the bound is ln N). This is the natural default for your per-category pools.

### 3. Sleeping/specialist experts solve partial coverage (M5, M7)
Your M5 structural nowcasts (weather/macro only) and M7 cross-venue signal (only where Kalshi/Metaculus quote the same event) are "sleeping experts" — they only forecast on some rounds. The canonical fixes are Freund–Schapire–Singer–Warmuth (1997) "Using and combining predictors that specialize" and the Chernov–Vovk (2009) device of attributing an inactive specialist a forecast equal to the learner's own aggregate, so a sleeping model's weight neither grows nor decays while it abstains. Regret is then measured only over rounds where the expert is awake (Blum & Mansour 2007; Kleinberg–Niculescu-Mizil–Sharma 2010, "Regret bounds for sleeping experts and bandits").

### 4. A virtual capital process IS an anytime-valid test — connecting to your existing CS
Shafer's "testing by betting" and Shafer–Vovk game-theoretic probability recast hypothesis testing as a wealth-accumulation game: an e-value is a nonnegative statistic with expectation ≤ 1 under the null; a test martingale is a running capital process from repeated fair bets against the null; Ville's inequality gives P(∃t: Wₜ ≥ 1/α) ≤ α — i.e., type-I error control under arbitrary optional stopping/peeking (Ramdas et al. 2023; Waudby-Smith & Ramdas 2024; Grünwald et al. 2024). Your Waudby-Smith–Ramdas confidence sequences and champion/challenger promotion are therefore already a virtual-wagering system; Phase 14 just visualizes the capital process and can literally use "challenger's bankroll vs champion's bankroll crossed the 1/α threshold" as the CI-gated promotion rule. Production deployments confirm this is battle-tested: Netflix (sequential A/B via Howard–Ramdas confidence sequences) and Adobe Experience Platform (Maharaj et al. 2023, "Anytime-Valid Confidence Sequences in an Enterprise A/B Testing Platform") both use it precisely to allow continuous peeking without type-I inflation.

### 5. Numerai is the largest real-world "crypto currency rewarding models" — and documents the failure modes
Numerai has participants stake NMR on weekly model submissions; models are scored on correlation (CORR) with 20-day-forward stock returns and Meta-Model Contribution (MMC = the unique, originality-rewarding contribution to the stake-weighted meta-model, SWMM). Positive scores earn NMR; negative scores burn staked NMR. The token itself is deflationary by design: the "NMR 2.0" contract upgrade (audited by Trail of Bits) reduced the maximum supply from 21 million to **11 million** and disabled minting ("NMR can no longer be minted — only burned… All 11 million of the tokens have now been minted"). Documented failure modes and design lessons directly relevant to Phase 14:
- **Gaming/exploits forced repeated redesigns.** Numerai discontinued its leaderboard bonus (from Sept 9, 2020) partly because of a documented exploit; per the forum thread "Leaderboard Bonus Exploit Uncovered," it was "susceptible to certain attacks by bad-actors" — the "p1p exploit" by which "a bad-actor could game the tournament and guarantee high payouts for himself with minimal risk." Payout schemes have changed many times (CORR → CORR+MMC → TC → announced MMC-only on a new "Teager" target).
- **MMC "punishes originality" during meta-model burns** (a documented forum critique): when the meta-model has a negative period, the originality-rewarding term can penalize the very diversification it is meant to reward.
- **MMC is zero-sum** (the sum of all participants' MMC nets to ~0), so a pure-MMC payout requires Numerai to place large stakes on internal models for participants to earn positive-sum — a structural subtlety.
- **Volatility of payouts** was a primary complaint (staked NMR swings sharply), motivating longer scoring windows.
The lesson: correlation-of-contribution payouts are exploitable and must be hardened; a wealth/stake mechanism amplifies both signal and gaming pressure.

### 6. Metaculus scoring design: proper, but with documented alignment problems
Metaculus used cumulative "Metaculus points" as its main score until late 2023, then switched (November 2023) to Baseline scores (vs. an impartial chance baseline, summed — rewarding volume) and Peer scores (log-score difference vs. all other forecasters, averaged — rewarding margin; the Peer score has the property that "on any given question, the sum of all participants' Peer scores is always 0"). Both are strictly proper. Documented pitfalls (Aguirre primer; "Alignment Problems With Current Forecasting Platforms," arXiv:2106.11248): positive-sum binary questions where you can gain points on any resolution; and a "copy the community median" strategy — confirmed by Metaculus staff ("if you copy the CP at all times you would indeed receive a high Baseline Accuracy score. The CP is generally a great forecast!") — that can reach the top of the volume leaderboard with little independent skill. The design lesson for Phase 14: separate a *volume/coverage* metric from a *per-decision margin* metric, and beware metrics that reward mimicking the market baseline (your M0).

### 7. Monetary framing for interpretability — best practices and pitfalls
- **Cumulative wealth curves per model on a log scale** (so equal geometric growth looks like equal slope), **drawdown charts**, and **"if you had bet $X" framing** are the standard vocabulary (Manifold leagues rank by realized profit with promotion/relegation between tiers; sports CLV dashboards like Pikkit, OddsJam, and Unabated).
- **Closing Line Value (CLV) is the sports world's exact analogue of your paired-vs-market Brier**: it measures whether you beat the market's final price, is regarded as the single best signal that separates skill from luck ("It's the closest thing sports betting has to a skill metric"), but requires large samples. Practitioner thresholds diverge sharply and should both be shown: Pikkit cites "At least 200-300 tracked bets before drawing conclusions," while more conservative shops (Betstamp, Doc's Sports) argue results "only start to show more signal than noise, north of 2,000 to 3,000 wagers." This validates your paired-vs-market design and warns hard about small-n readouts.
- **Attribution by contributing model / category / venue** can use Shapley values (each model is a "player"; marginal contribution to ensemble accuracy averaged over orderings), which is now standard for ensemble forecasting (mortality-model ensembles, electricity-price ensembles, load forecasting; e.g., "A Shapley value-based dynamic ensemble framework"). A simpler linear P&L attribution suffices when the ensemble is linear in model log-odds.
- **Pitfalls**: wealth variance ≫ skill signal; one lucky streak dominates a linear-scale visual; must show uncertainty via bootstrap wealth bands (reuse your event-level cluster bootstrap). Manifold's own history shows a systematic "Yes-bias" and even leaderboard-profit-calculation bugs — monetary leaderboards invite over-interpretation.

### 8. Faster adaptation: what theory and quant practice actually support
- **Small-η MWU is a form of safe continuous adaptation.** The regret bounds hold for any η, and small η means the weights move slowly and conservatively; the fixed-horizon optimal η = √(8 ln N/T) is precisely the *small* learning rate that trades quick reaction for stability. (Notably, this is the opposite of stochastic-gradient practice, where a larger step is balanced by Polyak–Ruppert averaging; in the expert setting Hedge deliberately uses a *smaller* learning rate.)
- **Fast adaptation on noisy binary outcomes overfits to streaks.** Financial ML routinely has R² of order 10⁻⁴; "anything beyond linear regression poses a significant overfitting risk," and low signal-to-noise means you need far more data to validate a signal. Updating model internals per-outcome invites gambler's-fallacy dynamics — exactly what your "no code path adjusts a model in response to an individual outcome" guardrail prevents.
- **Event-triggered refits are the principled middle ground.** CUSUM (Page 1954) and Bayesian Online Changepoint Detection (Adams & MacKay 2007) maintain a probabilistic run-length posterior; a rising change probability can trigger an off-schedule refit rather than waiting for the monthly calendar. BOCPD "does not give you a binary 'changepoint detected' flag — you get a probability that rises as evidence accumulates, and you choose the threshold at which to act," which fits your dry-run-by-default philosophy.
- **Anytime-valid CS already solves the peeking problem for promotion**, so challengers *can* be promoted faster and safely — the moment the confidence sequence excludes zero (or the challenger's capital crosses 1/α), regardless of how often you looked (Howard–Ramdas 2021, "Time-uniform, nonparametric, nonasymptotic confidence sequences").

### 9. Honest scale assessment: the statistics are unforgiving at dozens/week
Distinguishing near-equal forecasters requires large samples. MacKay (2025) shows that over ~100 questions, two forecasters whose probabilities typically differ by ~0.1 are only distinguishable (>95%) if their mean scores differ by more than ~0.02 ("The hypothesis that there is no difference … begins to look highly implausible … when, over 100 questions, and for forecasters whose probabilities typically differ by about 0.1, their mean scores differ by more than 0.02"). Thorp's classic result (Thorp 2006, via MacLean–Thorp–Ziemba, "Good and bad properties of the Kelly criterion"): distinguishing a game with a 1.0% edge from one with a 1.1% edge "takes two million trials to have an 84% chance that game A dominates game B." Wilks (2010): "quite large sample sizes, i.e. n > 1000, are required for higher-skill forecasts of relatively rare events." At dozens of resolutions/week, wealth curves for two decent models will remain statistically entangled for months. **Implication: the virtual economy's honest value is interpretability, disciplined selection, and drift detection — not a large, quickly-verifiable accuracy gain.**

## Details

### A. The wagering rule (per-model virtual bankroll)
For each frozen forecast, model i quotes probability pᵢ against market YES price q. The Kelly fraction for a binary contract paying $1 is:

**f\* = (p·b − (1−p)) / b, where b = (1−q)/q** (net odds implied by the market price).

Equivalently, for a YES bet, **f\* = (p − q) / (1 − q)**. Use **fractional Kelly (your existing 0.2×)** with a **per-position cap (5%)** and your existing entry gates (edge ≥ 0.05, spread ≤ 0.03, depth ≥ $500, 0.05 < p < 0.95). Each model's virtual bankroll evolves multiplicatively: on resolution, Wᵢ ← Wᵢ·(1 + fᵢ·outcome_return). Critically, for *model comparison* the bankroll should bet against the **market price** (this is the CLV/paired-Brier analogue and isolates skill vs. the M0 baseline), and every model bets the same frozen slate for apples-to-apples comparison. Full Kelly has a documented ~33% probability of halving the bankroll before doubling it (MacLean, Ziemba & Blazenko 1992, "Growth versus security in dynamic investment analysis," *Management Science* 38: 1562–1585; half-Kelly cuts that to ~1/9 while retaining ~75% of full-Kelly growth). Never run virtual bankrolls at full Kelly even in simulation — it makes the visuals dominated by variance.

### B. The weighting rule (MWU on ensemble weights only)
Maintain a weight wᵢ per model. After each resolution with realized outcome y, incur each model's log loss ℓᵢ = −[y·ln pᵢ + (1−y)·ln(1−pᵢ)] and update:

**wᵢ ← wᵢ · exp(−η·ℓᵢ)**, then renormalize so Σwᵢ = 1.

- With **η = 1 and log loss, this is exactly Bayesian model averaging** with regret ≤ ln N vs. the best model (Vovk 2001). This is the natural default and reconciles cleanly with your per-category log-odds pooling: run one MWU pool **per category**, initialized from your current fitted weights as the prior.
- For robustness at small n, optionally use a **smaller η** (e.g., η = √(8 ln N/T) with T a horizon of a few hundred), plus a **weight floor** (wᵢ ≥ w_min, e.g., 0.02) so no model is ever fully killed, and a **weight cap** (wᵢ ≤ w_max, e.g., 0.5) so no model dominates from a lucky streak. The floor/cap also implement your "bounded step per cycle" guardrail on the mixture.
- **Correlation discount**: keep your existing correlation-discounted extremization. MWU's regret guarantee is against the best *single* expert and implicitly assumes distinct experts; M1/M1.x/M2 are correlated. Either apply MWU to decorrelated residual signals or keep your extremization step downstream of the MWU mixture.

### C. Sleeping experts (M5, M7, and any category-limited model)
On rounds where model i does not forecast, do NOT update its weight against a fabricated loss. Use the Chernov–Vovk convention: assign the sleeping model the aggregate's own prediction (so its multiplicative factor is 1 and its share is preserved), and renormalize only over awake models for the actual mixture. This yields the correct "regret over awake rounds" guarantee and means M5's weight reflects only its weather/macro performance, not its silence elsewhere.

### D. e-process for champion/challenger promotion
Frame promotion as: challenger C vs. incumbent champion H. Define a per-event betting factor on the null "C is no better than H." A simple test martingale multiplies, per resolved event, by a factor derived from the log-likelihood ratio of the two models' forecasts (bet proportional to C's edge over H). Capital Wₜ starts at 1; promote when Wₜ ≥ 1/α (e.g., 1/0.05 = 20); this is anytime-valid, so you may check every day without inflating error. This literally implements your CI-gated promotion as a virtual wager and unifies it with the bankroll visuals. Keep the automatic rollback circuit breaker: if a promoted challenger's capital process against the old champion reverts below 1, roll back.

### E. Dashboard specification
1. **Per-model equity curves** (log scale), betting the frozen slate vs. market, with **bootstrap wealth bands** (event-level cluster bootstrap, matching your CIs).
2. **Drawdown panel** per model and for the ensemble.
3. **Weight-evolution stacked area** (the MWU mixture over time, per category), annotated with any event-triggered refits.
4. **Attribution waterfall**: ensemble P&L decomposed by model (Shapley or linear-log-odds contribution), by category, by venue.
5. **CLV-style readout**: % of markets where each model beat the market price, plus mean edge — with a visible "n too small" flag until per-cell n crosses a threshold (borrow the sports 200–300-bet practitioner floor, and treat rankings as provisional until n approaches the ~2,000+ significance regime, adapted to your MDE).
6. **e-process panel**: champion-vs-challenger capital with the 1/α promotion line and the rollback line.
7. **Sports null control** shown as a flat/zero-skill reference bankroll to calibrate the eye against noise.

### F. Library recommendations (solo Python stack)
- **NumPy/Pandas** for the ledger, bankroll accounting, and MWU (the update is three lines — you likely don't need a framework).
- **River** (online-ml/river) if you want a maintained streaming-ML abstraction for the expert-weighting loop (built for one-sample-at-a-time updates, dict-based inputs, concept-drift tooling).
- **ruptures** or a small **BOCPD** implementation (Adams–MacKay) for changepoint-triggered refits; `bayesian_changepoint_detection` packages exist.
- **SHAP** only if you want game-theoretic attribution; for a linear-in-log-odds ensemble, a hand-rolled Shapley over ≤8 models (2⁸ = 256 coalitions) is trivial and exact — no library needed.
- **confseq** (Waudby-Smith/Howard/Ramdas) for anytime-valid confidence sequences / e-processes, matching your existing WSR machinery.
- **matplotlib/arviz** for the dashboards; bootstrap bands from your existing cluster-bootstrap code.

### G. What faster cadence would break (explicit)
- **LLM components (M3) are live-only**: MWU on ensemble weights is fine because it never re-invokes the LLM on resolved history; but do NOT let any faster loop attempt to backtest or re-score M3 — that violates your leakage constraint. M3's weight can update from live forward performance only.
- **Bounded-step guarantee**: enforce it as a per-cycle cap on |Δwᵢ| (e.g., ≤ 20% relative) on top of MWU; small η already yields this, but cap explicitly.
- **Challenger promotion statistics**: anytime-valid CS means peeking is already solved, so weekly (or daily) promotion checks are statistically safe — this is the one place faster cadence is unambiguously fine and beneficial.
- **Rollback circuit breaker**: a faster weight loop must live inside the same breaker — if ensemble Brier vs. market degrades past a threshold, freeze weights at last-good and revert to the monthly-fitted mixture.
- **No per-decision model-internal updates**: the MWU loop touches only the *mixture weights over frozen model outputs*, never a model's parameters, preserving your hardest guardrail.

## Recommendations

**Stage 1 (ship first — pure interpretation, zero control risk).** Implement per-model virtual bankrolls (0.2× Kelly, 5% cap) betting the frozen slate against the market price, plus the dashboard (equity curves with bootstrap bands, drawdowns, CLV readout, attribution waterfall, sports-null reference). This is read-only, aligns with your architecture, and delivers the intuitive skill visualization immediately. **Benchmark to change course:** if per-model bankroll curves stay inside each other's bootstrap bands after ~3 months, treat rankings as not-yet-significant and keep decisions on the monthly fit.

**Stage 2 (weekly MWU on weights only, dry-run first).** Run the log-loss MWU pool per category in **shadow mode** alongside the monthly fit for at least 8 weeks. Use η=1 (Bayesian-mixture default) with weight floor 0.02, cap ~0.5, and an explicit ≤20% relative per-cycle step cap. Initialize from the current fitted weights. Promote MWU from shadow to live only if its out-of-sample paired Brier beats the monthly fit's with a confidence sequence excluding zero. **Threshold:** live-switch requires the anytime-valid CS on ΔBrier(MWU − monthly) to exclude 0.

**Stage 3 (event-triggered refits).** Add BOCPD/CUSUM on per-category rolling Brier (or on the market-vs-model divergence). When change probability crosses a threshold (start ~0.5, tune for false-alarm rate), trigger an off-schedule "lab learn" — still walk-forward, bounded-step, champion/challenger-gated, dry-run by default. This gives regime responsiveness without abandoning the monthly discipline.

**Stage 4 (unify promotion as e-process).** Re-express champion/challenger promotion as a capital process with a 1/α threshold, checked as often as you like (anytime-valid). Wire the rollback breaker to the same process.

**Do NOT:** let any loop update model internals per outcome; backtest M3; run full Kelly even in simulation; or present a monetary leaderboard without bootstrap bands and n-flags.

## Caveats
- **Scale honesty**: at dozens of resolutions/week, wealth-based selection converges slowly (MacKay 2025; Thorp 2006; Wilks 2010). Expect the economy to be decision-useful for interpretation and drift-detection long before it can *statistically prove* one good model beats another. Treat visually dramatic bankroll gaps with suspicion until bootstrap bands separate.
- **Wealth-vs-log-score is not a free lunch**: because Kelly log-wealth equals log-score in expectation (both KL-governed), wealth-based weighting does not converge *faster* than direct log-loss weighting — I found no source demonstrating a small-n speed advantage for either; they are the same object rescaled. Choose wealth framing for interpretability, log-loss MWU for the actual weighting math.
- **Gaming/robustness**: Numerai's history shows contribution/wealth payouts are exploitable and required repeated redesign. Since your system has no external adversaries (models are yours), gaming risk is low, but the same math (MMC punishing originality during meta-model burns; zero-sum contribution) warns against naïve "contribution" weighting during ensemble-wide bad streaks.
- **Correlated experts**: MWU/Hedge regret guarantees assume the comparator is the best single expert; your models are correlated (M1/M1.x/M2 share structure), so keep the correlation-discount/extremization step and consider MWU on residualized signals.
- **Metaculus/Manifold lessons**: proper scoring still admits "copy the baseline" and volume-gaming strategies; ensure your metrics don't inadvertently reward models that merely track M0.
- **Some cited practitioner figures are secondary** (CLV sample-size rules of thumb, Manifold Brier scores) and come from vendor/community sources rather than peer review; the academic core (Cover, Vovk, Cesa-Bianchi & Lugosi, Shafer & Vovk, Ramdas et al., Kelly/Thorp/MacLean–Ziemba) is solid and primary.
