# 4. Methodology: The Measurement Instrument

*Draft v1, 2026-07-11. Status: results-independent — safe to finalize before the data freeze. Numbers in [brackets] are placeholders to be filled at analysis time. Companion documents: `docs/pre_analysis_plan.md` (frozen 2026-07-06, addenda 9.1–9.3), CLAUDE.md v2.7 (system specification), and the replication repository.*

---

The contribution of this section is the instrument itself. Empirical work on prediction-market calibration is, almost without exception, retrospective: the researcher downloads resolved history and fits hypotheses to it, and the reader must trust that the hypotheses preceded the data. We invert this arrangement. Every forecast analyzed in this paper was frozen in an append-only ledger before the corresponding market resolved; the ledger's daily state is cryptographically committed to a public repository; and the hypotheses, primary outcome, exclusion rules, and analysis plan were fixed in a dated pre-analysis plan before the confirmatory window opened. The design questions this section answers are: what exactly counts as "skill" against a market price (§4.1); how the commitment device works and what it does and does not guarantee (§4.2); what it means to pre-register a system that is permitted to update itself (§4.3); how skill is scored and how inference remains valid under continuous monitoring (§4.4–4.5); how selection and contamination are controlled (§4.6); how the harness itself is tested (§4.7); and how statistical significance is separated from economic significance (§4.8). Robustness pre-specifications and the replication procedure close the section (§4.9–4.10).

## 4.1 Identification: what "beating the market" means

A forecasting system can look impressive against weak baselines. Resolved binary markets are typically imbalanced, so accuracy against chance, against a constant base rate, or against a naive classifier is uninformative. The only comparison that carries economic content is against the market's own contemporaneous price: the price is simultaneously the best-known public aggregate and the thing a skilled forecaster would trade against.

Our unit of measurement is therefore the *paired* difference in proper scores. For forecast $i$ on a binary market, let $p^m_i$ denote the market mid-price captured at the forecast timestamp, $\hat{p}_i$ the model's probability frozen at the same timestamp, and $y_i \in \{0,1\}$ the resolved outcome. Using the Brier score $B(p, y) = (p - y)^2$ (Brier, 1950; Gneiting & Raftery, 2007), define the paired difference

$$d_i \;=\; B(p^m_i, y_i) - B(\hat{p}_i, y_i) \;=\; (p^m_i - \hat{p}_i)\,(p^m_i + \hat{p}_i - 2 y_i).$$

Positive $d_i$ means the model beat the market on that observation. *Skill* is the mean of $d_i$ over resolved forecasts, and every skill number in this paper is a paired quantity of this form, computed per venue against that venue's own price. Pairing is not a stylistic choice: between-market variation in question difficulty is the dominant variance component of raw scores, and it cancels exactly in $d_i$. The same statistic underlies the closing-line-value tradition in sports-market evaluation and the forecaster-comparison framework of Choe & Ramdas (2024), which we adopt for sequential inference in §4.5.

Two consequences of this definition discipline the rest of the design. First, the market price must be captured *at* the forecast timestamp and stored with the forecast — a stale comparison price manufactures fake skill in either direction; our freshness rule ([15] minutes for the liquid tier, 90 for the tail) makes a forecast ineligible rather than allowing a stale pair. Second, log loss is computed alongside the Brier score throughout, and the two never disagreed on any qualitative conclusion reported below; we present Brier in the main text for its variance decomposition (§4.5) and report log-loss tables in the appendix.

## 4.2 The forecast ledger and the commitment device

The ledger is append-only in an enforced sense, not an aspirational one: the storage layer registers an authorizer that rejects `UPDATE` and `DELETE` statements against the forecast table, and the test suite asserts that such statements raise. Each row freezes, at write time: the model identifier, the probability, the market it concerns, the market price and spread captured at the same instant, and an `inputs_hash`. We describe the hash precisely rather than gesture at it, since precision here is the entire point of the mechanism: it is a SHA-256 digest over exactly four fields — the model identifier, the fitted artifact's version tag, the two configuration sections that can affect a forecast (`forecast`, `m3`), and the snapshot timestamp the price was read from. It is deliberately narrower than "everything that could matter" — code version is tracked elsewhere in the system but not folded into this particular hash, and it covers one snapshot timestamp per forecast rather than a per-input identifier for every joined data source. What it buys is still real: given the repository at the recorded artifact version and the two logged configuration sections, the price and probability are recomputable and checkable against what was actually written. Venue is not a column on this table at all — it is carried by the market it references, and analyses that need it join on the market's own venue field. Two additional pieces of information some readers may expect on this row are not present as stored columns: a model's fitted "version" beyond its identifier lives in the separate version registry (§4.3), not on the forecast row itself, and the time-to-resolution bucket used by the recalibration family (§5.2) is reconstructed from the recorded timestamp and the market's resolution date rather than stored as its own field.

Individual auditability does not by itself establish *when* a forecast existed. For that, a nightly job computes a SHA-256 digest over the day's appended forecast rows and commits the digest, together with the row range and date, to the public repository (`docs/ledger_commitments.jsonl`). The commitment inherits its timestamp from the public git history, which the author cannot silently rewrite; digests are additionally anchored via [OpenTimestamps / not used]. Verification is anchored to the committed row-id range rather than a live re-query by date, so a forecast that arrives late for an already-committed day can never silently change what that day's commitment covers. The commitment chain therefore establishes, without trusting the author, that the analyzed forecasts existed in their stated form before the outcomes they predict were known.

We state the scheme's limits as plainly as its guarantees. Commitments begin on 2026-07-06; forecasts recorded before the first commitment enjoy no pre-registration protection and are used only for model fitting, never for confirmatory claims (PAP §7 — the pre-commitment history is not retroactively hashed, because a retroactive hash would certify nothing). The scheme proves precedence, not correctness: a committed forecast can still be wrong, and a committed analysis plan can still be violated — which is why the plan itself, not only the data, is under version control with a frozen body and dated addenda (§4.3). Finally, commitments cover the ledger, not the collector: a gap in data collection is visible as a gap, and derived metrics that span recorded gaps are excluded and counted rather than interpolated.

## 4.3 Pre-registering a system that updates itself

A static pre-registration would be dishonest about what this system is: model parameters are refit monthly, an ensemble reweights, and challenger versions can replace incumbents. Freezing the parameters would test a strawman; letting them drift silently would void the pre-registration. Our resolution is that **the pre-registered object is the system together with its update protocol**, both fixed before the confirmatory window: refits occur only in a scheduled monthly batch run, only on walk-forward windows, with per-cycle parameter movement bounded, and every parameter set versioned in a registry with an immutable artifact hash. (The specification anticipates a second, changepoint-triggered off-schedule run under identical rules — a faster reaction to a genuine regime shift without loosening any of the governing constraints — but as of this writing that trigger is a documented design intention, not implemented code; the monthly cron is the only trigger this paper's confirmatory results can rely on, and this sentence will need to be revisited if that changes before the freeze.) A new version earns confirmatory track record only from forecasts made after its own registration timestamp; promotion from challenger to incumbent requires the anytime-valid interval of §4.5 to exclude zero, and a promoted version that subsequently underperforms is rolled back by a pre-specified circuit breaker. No code path adjusts any model in response to an individual outcome. The reader is thus never asked "which parameters were pre-registered?" — the answer is: the algorithm that produces them, its schedule, its step bounds, and its promotion gate, all of which are in the committed specification (CLAUDE.md §6, guardrails 14, 17–18).

The pre-analysis plan (committed 2026-07-06) fixes three primary hypotheses, the sole claim-bearing statistic, exclusion rules, and the honesty-tier vocabulary of §4.5. The plan's body is frozen; changes are possible only as dated, appended addenda, of which there are [three] at the time of writing: 9.1 fixes the analysis freeze at 2026-12-31 (committed 2026-07-09, three days into a six-month window, before any confirmatory resolution had accumulated); 9.2 corrects a methods citation (§4.5); 9.3 pre-specifies the settlement-discount robustness stratification (§4.9) in response to Gebele & Matthes (2026), committed before rather than after the data could speak to it.

## 4.4 Scoring, clustering, and the effective sample

All scoring is performed after resolution against the frozen pair $(\hat{p}_i, p^m_i)$. Resolutions are recorded with the venue's official outcome and a dispute flag ([UMA] dispute-window escalations on Polymarket; exchange determinations on Kalshi); disputed markets remain in the primary sample and are excluded in a pre-specified robustness pass (§4.9).

The observations are not independent, in two nested ways. Multiple forecasts on the same market across days share that market's single outcome; and — after the multi-venue expansion — the same underlying real-world event may be listed on several venues, so venue-market rows describing one event are one observation of the world, however many exchanges price it. We therefore cluster at the *event* level: markets are linked to a common event identifier through a human-confirmed cross-venue mapping (machine-proposed candidate pairs are never used unconfirmed, because one mismatched pair silently poisons both the cross-venue signal and the clustering), with the market identifier as the clustering fallback where no mapping exists. Descriptive confidence intervals are computed by a cluster bootstrap that resamples whole event clusters (Cameron & Miller, 2015, for the general treatment of cluster-robust inference); every reported $n$ counts resolved event clusters, not forecast rows and not venue-market listings. In our data, ignoring the venue dimension of the clustering would overstate the effective sample in the overlapping categories by a factor of [x.x].

## 4.5 Inference under continuous monitoring

The system scores itself nightly, and its operator — like any honest reader of a live dashboard — looks at the results as they accrue. Classical fixed-$n$ intervals are invalid under such monitoring: the probability that a 95% interval excludes zero *at some point* during a long null run far exceeds 5%. Rather than pretending the peeking does not happen, we make it statistically free. The claim-bearing statistic for every hypothesis is a **time-uniform confidence sequence** for the running mean of the paired differences $d_i$, ordered by resolution time and clustered as in §4.4: a sequence of intervals $\mathrm{CI}_t$ satisfying $\Pr(\forall t: \mu \in \mathrm{CI}_t) \ge 1-\alpha$, so that conclusions remain valid at every look time simultaneously, under optional stopping and optional continuation (Howard, Ramdas, McAuliffe & Sekhon, 2021; Ramdas et al., 2023).

Concretely, we use the normal-mixture uniform boundary of Howard et al. (2021), applied with a plug-in running sample variance. We flag the choice explicitly: this is an asymptotic variant — the cited paper's own guarantees are nonasymptotic, and the betting-based construction of Waudby-Smith & Ramdas (2024) provides exact nonasymptotic coverage for bounded variables at the cost of additional machinery. We verified the plug-in variant's empirical coverage on simulated nulls matching our data-generating process (100% of 500 simulated null runs contained zero across all look times; test suite, `test_anytime.py`), and we document the construction and its rationale in the released code (`eval/anytime.py`). The confidence sequence is not only the reporting device but the *decision* device: challenger promotion, rollback, and any skill claim in this paper cite it; the bootstrap intervals of §4.4 are descriptive.

Anytime-validity purchases its safety with width, so we pair it with two power disciplines that are also honesty disciplines. First, a **precision-weighted stratified estimator** exploits the known heteroskedasticity of the paired differences: conditional on the price pair, $\operatorname{Var}(d_i)$ is proportional to the outcome variance $p(1-p)$ at the market's price level, so price is a legitimate, externally defined stratification variable rather than a post hoc one. Forecasts are stratified into fixed price buckets on $p^m_i$; within each stratum with $n_s \ge 30$, the stratum mean $\bar{d}_s$ and its variance are computed; and strata are pooled with inverse-variance weights, $\widehat{\text{skill}}_{pw} = \sum_s w_s \bar d_s / \sum_s w_s$, $w_s = 1/\widehat{\operatorname{Var}}(\bar d_s)$. The estimator has a built-in degeneracy check that we enforce as a unit test: under homogeneous within-stratum variance it collapses *exactly* to the raw pooled mean, so any divergence between the two estimators is attributable to genuine variance heterogeneity, not to the weighting scheme itself. A confirmatory claim requires the anytime-valid sequence and the stratified estimator's bootstrap interval to exclude zero *and* agree in direction; fewer than three qualifying strata yields "insufficient data for the stratified check" rather than a number.

Second, every skill figure is published next to its own **minimum detectable effect**, $\mathrm{MDE} \approx 2.8\,\hat{\sigma}_d/\sqrt{n}$ (80% power at $\alpha = 0.05$), with $n$ in event clusters, and carries one of three pre-registered tier labels: $n < 200$ "insufficient data," $200 \le n < 500$ "preliminary," $n \ge 500$ standard claim. A point estimate smaller than its own MDE is noise by construction and is labeled as such regardless of how suggestive it looks. Reporting windows are fixed in advance (full confirmatory window and trailing 90 days); no other windows are computed, which removes window-shopping as a degree of freedom.

## 4.6 Selection, contamination, and look-ahead controls

Selection effects are controlled at the universe level, not the analysis level. Inexpensive models forecast the *entire* eligible universe every day; no market is chosen or dropped because it "looks forecastable." Universe eligibility rules (category priorities; exclusion of self-referential crypto price-target markets; exclusion of markets already priced beyond [0.95/0.05] as forecast targets) were fixed in the pre-analysis plan, and every excluded market is logged with a machine-readable reason code and date, so the analyzed sample is reconstructible and auditable rather than merely described.

The one model with a per-forecast marginal cost — the LLM evidence pipeline — is confined to a deterministic liquidity-ranked top-$K$, with a pre-specified, seeded randomization at the boundary: markets ranked $K\pm10$ are randomly assigned into or out of coverage, the seed is logged, and the assignment is exactly reproducible. Specified and implemented as it is, this randomization is **not currently enabled** in the running configuration; until it is, M3's marginal contribution is a correlational rather than a designed estimand, and enabling it before the freeze is a precondition for the causal framing that follows, not a detail that can be assumed (§5.7 flags the same gap where M3 itself is described). Once enabled, the randomization converts the pipeline's marginal contribution from a correlation into an estimand with a design (§[Results]); it is compatible with the no-editorial-selection rule because a logged coin flip embodies no judgment. Two further contamination controls apply to this component: it is evaluated *live only* — never backtested, because resolved outcomes of past events plausibly sit in the underlying model's training data — and, like every component, it earns confirmatory track record only after its version's registration timestamp. Look-ahead is excluded mechanically for all models: a forecast may join only input rows whose published timestamps precede the forecast timestamp, and the joined snapshot identifiers are frozen into `inputs_hash`, making any violation detectable after the fact.

## 4.7 Testing the harness: negative control and validity diagnostics

A measurement instrument needs its own null. Sports markets — where our models have no plausible informational or structural edge over deep, professionally traded prices — are forecast by the same pipeline, scored by the same code, and reported in the same tables, with the pre-registered expectation of zero skill. The null control functions as a continuously running placebo: apparent "skill" there indicates a broken harness (a leakage path, a scoring bug, a clustering error) and invalidates concurrent claims pending investigation, a condition written into the pre-analysis plan rather than decided upon ex post. The same control anchors a secondary diagnostic: pre-resolution price drift toward a model's view (a closing-line-value analogue) is trusted as a leading indicator only while it shows no "skill" on the null control. This CLV diagnostic is, at present, the one place gap-awareness reaches into scoring: a drift window overlapping a recorded collection gap (§3.3) is excluded and counted rather than silently treated as absent data. We state the scope precisely rather than imply a general guarantee: the primary skill statistics — Brier, log loss, and the RPS extension — carry no equivalent gap-exclusion logic of their own and are computed directly from whatever ledger rows exist; a genuine outage's effect on them is bounded by however few forecasts it prevented from ever being written, not by an explicit downstream filter.

## 4.8 Economic versus statistical significance

A statistically resolvable calibration edge can be economically empty. To bound economic content without making trading claims, a simulated portfolio — labeled as simulation everywhere it appears — applies fractional Kelly sizing with a per-position cap and conservative entry gates (minimum edge [0.05], maximum spread [0.03], minimum book depth [$500], prices within [0.05–0.95]) to the frozen forecasts, fills against the recorded order-book snapshots with simulated slippage, and charges venue fees from a *versioned, sourced* fee schedule (`data/fee_schedule.yaml`; sourced 2026-07-09 from each venue's own published fee documentation, not invented). Both venues price fees as a function of the fill price rather than a flat rate — $\text{fee} = \text{stake} \times \text{rate} \times (1 - p)$, peaking near even odds and vanishing toward the tails — with Polymarket's rate varying by category ([0.03] sports to [0.07] crypto, [0] for geopolitics) and Kalshi charging a flat [0.07] coefficient in the same formula. Two limitations of this accounting are stated rather than smoothed over. First, both venues' schedules are recorded as their currently-published rates; Kalshi's effective date (2026-07-07) is stated on its own fee page, but Polymarket's documentation does not state when its current structure took effect, so its `effective_from` field is an explicit sentinel meaning "at least as far back as our data," not a researched historical fact — we do not claim to know whether or when either schedule changed prior to that date. Second, the simulation always executes as a taker (it crosses the book rather than resting an order), so only taker fees are charged; both venues also charge or rebate makers differently, and that side of the fee structure is not modeled here because our simulated strategy never plays it. The resulting net-of-cost line is reported alongside every headline skill figure. We emphasize the direction of inference: the simulation can demonstrate that an edge does *not* survive frictions; it cannot demonstrate that live trading would capture it.

## 4.9 Pre-specified robustness

Three robustness passes were committed before the data could inform them. (i) *Disputed resolutions:* PAP Addendum 9.2(b) commits that all primary analyses will be re-run excluding markets whose resolution was formally disputed. We flag a design point worth resolving before that pass is run, rather than discovering it while writing Results: at present, disputed markets are already excluded unconditionally, everywhere, by a hard filter applied in every scoring query — there is currently no code path that *includes* them, so a "with vs. without disputed markets" comparison has nothing to compare against yet and would trivially reproduce the primary result if run today. Delivering on Addendum 9.2(b) as intended requires an additional, disputed-inclusive scoring path built specifically for this comparison; we note this as an implementation item to close before the pass is run, not as a change to what was committed. (ii) *Settlement discounting:* Gebele & Matthes (2026) show that a substantial share of apparent long-horizon underconfidence in near-certain contracts reflects a rational discount on capital locked until delayed settlement rather than belief error. Because our H1 lives on exactly that terrain, PAP Addendum 9.3 pre-commits a stratification of the H1 analysis by the two design features their mechanism identifies as wedge-compressing — negRisk convertibility and collateral-yield availability — so that if the measured effect survives *within* the low-wedge strata, settlement discounting is not doing the work, and if it does not, we will say so with the same prominence. (iii) *Per-venue splits:* every pooled result is reported per venue, since the hierarchical recalibration layer is designed to absorb venue-level differences and the reader should be able to see whether it did.

## 4.10 Reproducibility

The instrument, not only the data, is the replication package: the full system is open-source ([repository; archived release DOI]), with [415] automated tests covering the properties this section asserts — ledger immutability, look-ahead exclusion, clustering behavior, the stratified estimator's collapse invariant, confidence-sequence coverage on simulated nulls, and the promotion/rollback gates. A separate export command produces a resolved-forecast replication dataset alongside a manifest recording the code and schema versions, generation time, and row count — a description of the data, not itself a cryptographic check. The cryptographic check is the mechanism of §4.2: `docs/ledger_commitments.jsonl` records one SHA-256 digest per day, over that day's exact row-id range, committed and pushed to the public repository. Verification requires no cooperation from the author: (1) clone the repository; (2) for any committed date, recompute the SHA-256 digest over the exact forecast rows spanning that day's recorded id range; (3) compare against the digest committed on that date, whose timestamp is fixed by the public git history rather than by anything we assert.
