# 5. Models

*Draft v1, 2026-07-11. Status: results-independent. Numbers in [brackets] are placeholders or values to re-verify against configuration at the analysis freeze. Learning, versioning, and promotion mechanics are specified once, in §4.3, and are not repeated here; every model below is subject to them.*

---

The model roster is a set of *pre-specified corrections, each targeting one documented mechanism* — not a search over model space. This is a deliberate identification choice: when a named correction beats the market, the result says something about the mechanism it targets; when it fails, it bounds that mechanism's exploitability. Nothing in the roster was added, removed, or re-tuned in response to confirmatory-window results, and the roster itself is fixed in the committed specification. Three design rules apply uniformly. First, *determinism at forecast time*: given its frozen inputs, every model's output is exactly reproducible, and the inputs are pinned by the per-forecast hash of §4.2 (the one component with a stochastic element, the LLM pipeline, freezes its intermediate outputs so that everything downstream of them is deterministic). Second, *disclosed information sets*: the models are ordered below by the information they consume, from the market price alone to cross-venue prices and news text, so that any measured skill can be attributed to an information source. Third, *coverage by structure, not by judgment*: inexpensive models forecast the entire eligible universe; models with structural scope (a weather model has nothing to say about an election) or per-forecast cost are confined by deterministic rules and, at the cost boundary, by the seeded randomization of §4.6.

## 5.1 M0 — the market baseline

M0 records the venue mid-price at the forecast timestamp as its own probability and writes it to the ledger like any other model. Its paired skill is zero by construction; its purpose is threefold. It is the anchor that makes every other model's skill interpretable (§4.1); it is the carrier of the market's information within the ensemble, so that other components are estimated as *deviations from* the price rather than replacements for it; and it is the tautology check in every results table — a pipeline in which M0's measured skill differs from zero has a scoring bug, not an insight.

## 5.2 The recalibration family: M1 and M1.x

**M1 (horizon-aware, applied uniformly).** The best-documented candidate inefficiency in these markets is horizon-dependent miscalibration: prices far from resolution are compressed toward 0.5, with calibration slopes rising from ≈1 near resolution to ≈1.3 beyond a month (Le, 2026; the pattern's older ancestor is horizon-conditioned distortion on Intrade, Page & Clemen, 2013). At the tails, recent Polymarket evidence indicates a *reversed* favorite–longshot bias (Qin & Yang, 2026) — opposite in sign to the classical pattern (Snowberg & Wolfers, 2010) — which dictates the central design rule of this family: **no bias direction is hardcoded; every curve is fitted.** M1 is a logistic recalibration of the market price,

$$\ell(\hat{p}) \;=\; \alpha_{h} + \beta_{h}\,\ell(p^m), \qquad \ell(p) = \log\tfrac{p}{1-p},$$

fitted per time-to-resolution bucket $h$ ([<7d, 7–30d, 30–90d, >90d]) on pre-window resolved history. A fitted $\beta_h > 1$ extremizes long-horizon prices; $\beta_h < 1$ would shrink them; the data decide. Category-level fitting is a natural extension the architecture anticipates but does not currently implement; the reported curve is horizon-only. M1 applies to the entire eligible universe on every forecastable venue with a single, venue-agnostic curve — it is M1.x (below), not M1, that lets the curve vary by venue. Guards: an isotonic-regression sanity check on each refit, monthly refit cadence under the §4.3 protocol, versioned curve artifacts; the horizon bucket used at forecast time is reconstructed from the recorded time-to-resolution rather than stored as its own column, which is sufficient for H1's bucket-level analysis but worth noting for anyone querying the ledger directly.

**M1.x (hierarchical, venue-aware).** Extending recalibration across venues poses a pooling dilemma. Venue biases are not interchangeable — the trade-size effect that appears on Kalshi does not replicate on Polymarket (Le, 2026), and tail biases can be opposite in sign across market types — so naive pooling of venues into one fit would average away exactly the object under study. Fitting each venue in isolation, however, wastes shared structure and leaves thin venues with noisy curves. M1.x resolves this with hierarchical partial pooling in the Stein–shrinkage tradition (Efron & Morris, 1977): a single jointly-fitted model

$$\ell(\hat{p}) \;=\; (\alpha_g + \alpha_v) + (\beta_g + \beta_v)\,\ell(p^v),$$

per horizon bucket, where the global parameters $(\alpha_g, \beta_g)$ are estimated jointly with the venue offsets $(\alpha_v, \beta_v)$, the latter shrunk toward zero by a ridge penalty scaled $\propto 1/n_v$ — an empirical-Bayes compromise under which a venue with few observations in a bucket is pulled hard toward the global curve and a venue with many can diverge where its own data demands it. Estimation is penalized maximum likelihood ([scipy]); a fully Bayesian treatment is reserved for a periodic offline audit of the shrinkage strengths rather than the production path.

**A precise statement of what is fit on what, stated plainly rather than left to infer from the architecture:** the parameters are currently estimated on our historical bootstrap, which is Polymarket-only (the source dataset carries no venue field, so every training row defaults to the `polymarket` tag); Kalshi's and Metaculus's own resolved outcomes enter the pipeline only as the walk-forward *validation* set that gates promotion (§4.3), not as parameter-estimation input. The hierarchical machinery is general — it is written to fit on any mix of the three allowed venues (guardrail: never play-money or archival sources) and will do so as our own collection accumulates enough resolved Kalshi and Metaculus outcomes to train on directly — but as things stand today, the fitted venue offsets are meaningfully estimated for one venue, with the others sharing the global curve by construction rather than by an estimated, venue-specific adjustment. We report this rather than the aspirational version of the design, and note it again wherever it bears on interpretation (§4.9's per-venue robustness split will make the practical consequence, if any, visible directly).

A second correction of the same kind concerns implementation, not statistics: the hierarchical curve is read by two real forecasters that write to the ledger under their own model identifiers, `m1_hier@polymarket` and `m1_hier@kalshi`; `m1_hier@metaculus` is not a third forecaster instance in this sense — it is a bare recalibration call used internally where the Metaculus community prediction is recalibrated before entering M7 (§5.6) as a cross-venue input signal, never itself written to `forecasts`. All three labels do read one shared, versioned artifact (`m1_hier_curves`), promoted as a unit against its own prior version under the §4.3 gates — there is no per-venue contest against M1, and M1's own artifact is never a party to M1.x's promotion; the families compete only through the ensemble's weights (§5.8). Given the training composition just described, H1's confirmatory claim about M1.x is scoped to `m1_hier@polymarket` specifically (matching the pre-analysis plan's own wording), not to the hierarchical mechanism as a cross-venue abstraction — a scoping that is honest about data, not merely about pre-registration language. One caveat is owed the reader regardless: with few venues in play at all, between-venue variance components are weakly identified — textbook behavior for hierarchical models with few groups — so M1.x should be read as a regularized estimator with a defensible shrinkage direction, not as a well-identified decomposition of venue effects, even once training data does span more than one venue.

## 5.3 M2 — category base rates

M2 supplies the outcome-frequency information that pure price transformations cannot: the category-conditional resolved base rate, blended with the market prior in log-odds space at a small fixed weight ([0.15] — the same blend the LLM aggregator of §5.7 uses, by design, not coincidence). This is not a shrinkage estimator in the empirical-Bayes sense; there is no partial pooling toward a global rate. The actual small-sample safeguard is abstention: a category with fewer than [50] resolved observations does not receive an M2 forecast at all, rather than an unstable rate computed from too little data. Uniquely in the roster, M2 is permitted to consume provenance-tagged historical archives beyond our own collection (§4.6), because a base rate is a property of the world rather than of a venue's microstructure — the same reasoning that *forbids* those archives everywhere else — though at present only the HF bootstrap is actually wired in; the GJP and PredictIt archives named in the collection policy remain documented intentions, not implemented data sources, and this section should be revisited once (or if) they are. Its role is diversification: a weak, slowly-varying anchor whose ensemble weight the data set, expected to matter most in categories where prices are thin and resolution frequencies are stable.

## 5.4 M5 — structural nowcasts

Where a credible external model of the underlying quantity exists, using the market price as the prior is leaving information unused. M5 covers two such categories, with two genuinely different mechanisms — the "structural nowcast" label describes the category, not one shared algorithm. For *weather* threshold markets, it consumes a numerical ensemble forecast (Open-Meteo's Ensemble API, GFS-based; the member count is a property of the underlying model run rather than a value this system fixes) and computes $\Pr(\text{threshold exceeded})$ as the empirical fraction of members exceeding the market's threshold, Laplace-smoothed against degenerate all-or-nothing fractions at small ensemble sizes — a direct exceedance count, not a fitted error distribution. For *macro-release* markets, the mechanism is different and does involve fitting: it anchors on published nowcasts ([GDPNow, PCENOW] via FRED) and converts a nowcast-vs-outcome surprise distribution, fitted on the nowcasts' own point-in-time release history, into bucket probabilities. M5's scope is structural — it forecasts every market its adapters cover and nothing else — which is a coverage boundary defined by the existence of an external model, not by editorial judgment (§4.6). We note the bar it faces: in the macro category specifically, the raw market price is already a strong forecaster relative to professional benchmarks (Diercks, Katz & Wright, 2026), so M5's confirmatory question is not "is the market bad here" but "does a structural layer add anything to an already-good price."

## 5.5 M6 — the coherence scanner

Mutually exclusive and exhaustive outcome sets (the negRisk structure) must carry prices that sum to one; observed sums deviate. M6 monitors linked outcome sets and, when the deviation exceeds a fixed threshold ([3%] of price mass, a configuration constant rather than one derived from a fee schedule), emits forecasts *only for the incoherent legs*, pulling them toward the coherent joint solution implied by the set. A second, separate mechanism covers pairs that are logically linked without being a formal negRisk set — a curated `links.yaml` (empty at project start, populated as such pairs are identified) encodes conditional-probability bounds between two markets whose outcomes constrain each other. Both mechanisms consume only contemporaneous prices and the relevant logical constraint, never external information, which makes M6 the cleanest in the roster: any skill it shows is evidence that internal-consistency violations persist long enough to be informative. Its per-set coherence deviation is computed and stored as a covariate intended for the distributional-scoring layer (§4's RPS extension), but as of this writing that integration is implemented and unit-tested without yet being exercised on live reporting — worth confirming is live before the covariate is used in any analysis that depends on it.

## 5.6 M7 — the cross-venue signal

When the same real-world event trades on several venues, the venues are partially independent crowds, and documented lead–lag structure between them (Ng, Peng, Tao & Zhou, 2026) implies the lagging price can be improved by the leading one. M7 outputs, for markets with a confirmed cross-venue match, the equal-weight mean of the *external* venues' log-odds, with the extremization exponent of §5.8 applied to the pooled value as a whole rather than as a per-venue weight — the home venue's own price is deliberately excluded, since M0 already carries it and the ensemble should learn the trust split rather than have it imposed. Kalshi enters at its mid-price; Metaculus enters through its recalibrated community prediction (§5.2, via the bare `m1_hier@metaculus` recalibration call, not a forecaster of its own); play-money venues are excluded by the provenance rule. The known failure point is question matching: superficially identical questions can differ in resolution semantics, and a single mismatched pair silently poisons both this signal and the event-level clustering of §4.4. Matching is therefore conservative — candidate pairs may be proposed by tooling, but every pair is human-confirmed against side-by-side resolution criteria before it is used, the confirmed mapping file is the committed source of truth, and unconfirmed proposals are never forecast. As of this writing the confirmed set is [19] pairs, all Kalshi — the Metaculus matching path is implemented but not yet exercised on live data — and this count should be treated as a snapshot, not a target: roughly [5.5] months remain before the confirmatory freeze, over which it will almost certainly change materially. External prices are snapshotted at the forecast timestamp under the same freshness rule as everything else, and M7 is fully deterministic at forecast time.

## 5.7 M3 — the LLM evidence pipeline (exploratory)

M3 tests whether public news that has not yet diffused into the price can be extracted at scale by a language model. Its design subordinates the model to arithmetic: for each covered market, a retrieval step collects recent articles; the LLM's only task is *evidence extraction* into a strict schema — discrete evidence items with direction, strength, source reliability, and relevance (judged strictly against the market's quoted resolution criteria, so an on-topic article about the wrong specific condition scores low) — and a deterministic aggregator then maps them into a bounded log-odds increment on top of the market prior,

$$L \;=\; \ell(p^m) \;+\; \operatorname{clip}\!\left(\sum_j s_j;\; \pm c_{\text{total}}\right), \qquad s_j = k \cdot \text{strength}_j \cdot \text{reliability}_j \cdot \text{relevance}_j \cdot \operatorname{sign}(\text{direction}_j)\cdot e^{-\text{age}_j/\tau}, \qquad \hat{p} = \sigma(L),$$

with a single cap on the *total* shift, not on individual items — there is no per-item clipping, only the aggregate is bounded. **The LLM never writes the final probability**; every extracted dossier and aggregation trace is frozen for audit. Three constraints define M3's evidentiary status. It is evaluated *live only* — never backtested — because resolved outcomes of historical events plausibly appear in the underlying model's training data, making retrospective LLM evaluation unfalsifiable (cf. Halawi, Zhang, Yueh-Han & Steinhardt, 2024, on LLM forecasting relative to crowds); the current extraction model is [DeepSeek's `deepseek-chat`, recorded as `m3_evidence@deepseek`] — a detail worth stating plainly in Methods, since a reader checking the released `model_id` field against a paper that said "Claude" or "GPT" would rightly be confused. Its coverage is a deterministic liquidity top-[K], with a seeded boundary randomization (§4.6) specified and implemented but **disabled in the current configuration** — until enabled, M3's marginal contribution is a correlational, not designed, estimand, and this needs to change before the confirmatory window closes if the causal framing of §4.6 is to hold for M3 specifically. And it carries no primary hypothesis: M3 is declared exploratory in the pre-analysis plan, and nothing in H1–H3 depends on it. We include it because the question is live in the literature and because the harness around it — live-only, randomized-boundary (once enabled), version-gated — is itself a methodological proposal for how LLM forecasters can be evaluated honestly.

## 5.8 M4 — the ensemble

M4 combines the roster per category as a weighted log-odds pool with extremization:

$$\ell(\hat{p}_{\text{ens}}) \;=\; a_c \sum_{k \in \mathcal{A}} w_{k,c}\, \ell(\hat{p}_k), \qquad \sum_{k \in \mathcal{A}} w_{k,c} = 1,$$

where $\mathcal{A}$ is the set of models present on the given market (structural-scope models simply drop out of the pool where they do not forecast, with weights renormalized over the present set — the standard specialist/sleeping-expert convention; Freund, Schapire, Singer & Warmuth, 1997; Chernov & Vovk, 2009). Weights are fitted per category on the *full* history of resolved forecasts under the §4.3 protocol — not a rolling window, a design point worth flagging for the reader rather than leaving to be discovered by inspecting the query: as the confirmatory window lengthens, early and late resolutions carry equal weight in this fit unless a windowing scheme is added later. Weights are equal until [100] resolved observations exist in the category, and constrained to a [2%] floor and [60%] ceiling per model so that no short streak can concentrate the pool on one component. The extremization exponent $a_c \in [1.0, 2.5]$ compensates for the systematic underconfidence of averaged probability forecasts (Satopää et al., 2014; Baron et al., 2014) — but only to the degree the pooled sources are informationally distinct. Following the substitutable-signals analysis of Neyman & Roughgarden (2022), the fitted exponent is scaled by the effective source count

$$n_{\text{eff}} \;=\; \frac{m}{1 + (m-1)\,\bar\rho},$$

with $m$ the number of present sources and $\bar\rho$ the mean pairwise correlation of source log-odds estimated on matched events: highly correlated sources (the recalibration family and the raw price, for instance) must not be extremized as though independent. The same discount governs M7's internal pool.

The ensemble's weight vector is the one place where continuous adaptation is permitted, under a narrow and explicitly documented exception to the batch-only rule: a shadow challenger derives weights from the models' sleeping-expert-normalized *average* log-wealth (cumulative log-wealth divided by forecast count, §[wealth ledger] — not the raw cumulative sum, which would reward coverage rather than skill) by a multiplicative-weights update, $w_k \propto \exp(\eta_t\, \bar{W}_k)$ with the regret-optimal decaying rate $\eta_t = \sqrt{8 \ln N / t}$ (Cesa-Bianchi & Lugosi, 2006; with log loss and $\eta = 1$ the update reduces to Bayesian model averaging, Vovk, 2001), subject to the same floor/ceiling and with correlated models grouped under a joint ceiling. The exception is safe by construction rather than by hope: the update touches only meta-level mixture weights, never any model's internal parameters; it runs in shadow; and it reaches production only through the same anytime-valid promotion gate as every other challenger, after a pre-specified probation ([90] days and the standard honesty tier). One implementation detail is worth stating precisely rather than glossed: the challenger shares its incumbent's model identifier (`m4_weights`) rather than registering under a distinct one — its identity as a challenger lives in the version tag, not the model_id, which is why promotion for this specific challenger is a same-model-id version contest like any other, not a cross-model contest like M1.x vs. M1. As of this writing it has registered repeatedly under its own versioned tags, never promoted, and remains within its probation window — a live, evolving number by the time of the confirmatory freeze rather than a fixed fact worth hardcoding here.
